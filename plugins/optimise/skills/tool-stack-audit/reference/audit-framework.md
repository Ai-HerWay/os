# The Tool Stack Audit Framework

The standard every stack is held to. The aim is not a tidy spreadsheet. It is the smallest, sharpest stack that genuinely serves the student's goals, with the money made visible and the AI they already pay for put to work.

The guiding principle: **the best stack is the smallest one that does the job. Fewer tools, used well, beats more tools used barely.**

---

## What to capture for every tool

- **Name** and the **job** it does (in one line).
- **Category** so overlaps surface: Marketing and content, Sales and CRM, Admin and operations, Finance, Knowledge and files, Design and creative, Automation and connectors, Communication, AI workspace, Other.
- **Tier or plan** and **monthly cost** (the student's real figure, or a verified tier price marked as estimated).
- **Real usage**: daily, weekly, rarely, almost never. Self-reported, but probe gently: when did you last open it?

---

## The utilisation read

A tool is only worth its cost if it is used and used well. Judge two things:
1. **How much they use it** (frequency above).
2. **How much of it they use** (depth). A tool used daily at 10 percent of its capability is both a maximise opportunity and a possible overlap with something else.

Low frequency plus low depth is the clearest signal to cut. High frequency plus low depth is the clearest signal to maximise.

---

## The four lenses (every tool gets one verdict)

| Verdict | When it applies | What to give the student |
|---|---|---|
| **Keep** | Earns its place, used well, no overlap, serves a goal | A one-line confirmation it is pulling its weight |
| **Maximise** | Worth keeping, but underused | The two or three features or AI capabilities they are missing, from live research |
| **Streamline** | Overlaps another tool doing the same job | Which one to keep, why, and the saving from dropping the other |
| **Cut** | Unused, not earning its cost, or now covered by an AI tool they already pay for | The reason and the monthly saving |

A verdict is a prompt to act, not a judgement. The point is the move it points to.

---

## The pay-twice-for-AI check

The fastest money leak in 2026 is paying for AI twice. Run this every time:
- List which of the student's existing tools already include AI (their CRM, design tool, docs, email, notetaker often do).
- Confirm their **one main AI workspace** (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or Copilot) and whether work is being split across several without a reason.
- Flag any standalone AI subscription whose job is already covered by that main workspace or by AI built into a tool they keep.

See `ai-leverage-map.md` for what an AI workspace can and cannot absorb.

---

## The cost picture

- **Monthly total**: the sum of every tool's monthly cost.
- **Annual total**: monthly times twelve, because annual is the number that makes people act.
- **Monthly waste**: the sum of the monthly cost of everything marked cut, plus the saving from each streamline (the tool being dropped).
- **Projected monthly saving**: what the student would save if they followed the cut and streamline recommendations. Be conservative and honest. Never inflate a saving to make the audit look impressive.

---

## The security pass

A stack audit is also a quiet safety check. Run these and report each as ok, action, or unknown:
- Data sharing is turned off in the main AI tool's settings.
- Client or confidential data is separated by structure (a folder or project per client), not mixed into one space.
- Connections follow least privilege: each tool or connector only reaches what it genuinely needs.
- No passwords or payment details are stored inside an agent. Logins stay manual.
- If they handle truly sensitive data, a Team plan or a local model has been considered for that work.

---

## The 30-day plan

Do not ask anyone to fix everything at once. Pick the three changes with the biggest impact (usually the largest clear cut, the most confusing overlap, and the highest-value maximise), give each a date, and attach the dollar or time impact. Three real moves beat a long list that never happens.

---

## Honesty rules for the audit

- Research before you assert. Every feature or price claim needs a source and a current check.
- Never fabricate a saving, a feature, or a limitation.
- Stay vendor-neutral. No affiliate or sponsored picks.
- Match recommendations to the student's goals and technical comfort, not to an ideal of tooling.
- Re-run quarterly. Tools and prices change, and the stack should get smaller and sharper over time, not bigger.
