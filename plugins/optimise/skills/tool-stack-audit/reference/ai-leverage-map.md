# The AI Leverage Map

How to judge what a student's AI workspace (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or Copilot, with Cowork, Claude Code, or Codex) can now do that they may be paying a separate tool for. This is a lens, not a rule. Always check the student's actual AI tool, tier, and comfort before recommending they drop something, and research the specific tool before claiming it is replaceable.

The honest framing: AI has absorbed a lot of single-purpose tools, but not all of them. The win is usually fewer point tools feeding one AI workspace, not "cancel everything".

---

## Often absorbed by an AI workspace (check, then likely cut or downgrade)

These are categories where a capable AI workspace frequently does the job, so a standalone subscription is often a pay-twice situation:
- **General writing and copy tools** (Jasper, Copy.ai and similar): a main AI workspace usually covers this.
- **Summarising, research assistants, and "chat with your docs" tools**: often native to the AI workspace now.
- **Basic chatbot or prompt-library tools**: usually replaced by skills and projects inside the workspace.
- **Simple transcription and meeting-notes tools**: increasingly built into the AI workspace or the meeting platform itself, so a separate paid one may be redundant.
- **Light image generation and simple graphics**: often covered for non-brand work; see the caveats below.
- **Spreadsheet-analysis and "ask your data" add-ons**: an AI workspace can often do the analysis directly.

For each, the test is: does the student's specific AI workspace, at their tier, actually do this well enough for their goal? Research it, do not assume.

---

## Usually NOT replaced by an AI workspace (keep, even in a lean stack)

Be careful and honest. AI does not replace these for most people:
- **Systems of record**: CRM, accounting, payroll, the email-sending platform, the booking system. AI can draft and read, but the record and the sending infrastructure stay.
- **Payments, invoicing, and compliance tools**: never recommend replacing these with an AI chat.
- **Design at brand quality and scale** (a real design tool with brand assets): AI assists, it rarely replaces the studio file.
- **Scheduling and publishing infrastructure** (the thing that actually posts or sends on a schedule).
- **Storage, security, and identity** (cloud drive, password manager, two-factor).
- **Industry-specific or regulated software** where the data or workflow is the point.

A lean stack still has a handful of these. The goal is not zero tools. It is no duplicated tools and no paid AI doing what the workspace already does.

---

## Judging whether a tool is being maximised

A tool can be worth keeping and still be a maximise opportunity. Look for:
- **AI features they are not using.** Many tools the student already pays for added AI in the last year (their docs, CRM, design tool, notetaker). If they are paying a separate AI tool for what their existing tool now does, that is both a maximise and a pay-twice flag.
- **A tier mismatch.** They are on a higher tier than they use (downgrade), or a lower tier is blocking a feature that would let them drop another tool (a rare, justified upgrade).
- **Power-user workflows** the student has never set up: templates, automations, integrations, keyboard-fast actions. Name the two or three that fit their goals, from research.

---

## The connection point

The biggest leverage is usually not a new tool. It is connecting the tools they keep to their AI workspace so the AI can read and draft across them, which is exactly what the rest of the AI Operating System teaches. When you find a tool worth keeping, note whether connecting it to their AI workspace would multiply its value. That is the on-brand "maximise", and it costs nothing extra.
