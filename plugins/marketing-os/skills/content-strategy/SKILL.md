# Skill: Content Strategy

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill owns the strategic content blueprint for your business: the decision layer that sits above every individual post, newsletter, or video. It works for the person whose content it governs (a founder building authority, a professional building a personal brand inside a role, or someone in real life building a community around a cause). It owns the full chain from audience need to content pillar to platform to repurposing flow to posting rhythm to call to action, and it keeps every one of those choices tied back to one primary goal. It does not write the individual posts (other skills do that). It decides what is worth writing and why.

## 2. Governing principle

Audience need comes first, the business goal comes second, and the format comes last: never publish a piece that does not connect to a named pillar and serve the primary goal, no matter how clever the idea.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

Three established, named bodies of work underpin this skill.

**Documented strategy beats improvisation.** The Content Marketing Institute (with MarketingProfs), in its annual B2B Content Marketing Benchmarks survey of nearly 1,000 marketers (Outlook for 2025), found that marketers with a documented content strategy report success at materially higher rates than those without one, and that a leading reason strategies underperform is the absence of clear goals. That is the entire reason this skill exists as a written blueprint tied to a single goal, rather than a posting habit. Source: Content Marketing Institute and MarketingProfs, "B2B Content Marketing Benchmarks, Budgets, and Trends: Outlook for 2025".

**Content pillars create consistency and compounding.** Joe Pulizzi, founder of the Content Marketing Institute and author of "Epic Content Marketing" (2013) and "Content Inc." (2015), established the now-standard practice of building content around a defined niche and a small set of core themes published consistently over time. Pillars are how a brand stays recognisable across many posts and platforms: every piece aligns to a core theme, so the audience always knows what this person stands for. This is the named framework behind the Content Pillars section below. We cite it as the established best-practice framework rather than as a single controlled study.

**People hire content to do a job.** Clayton Christensen of Harvard Business School formalised Jobs to Be Done (articulated in his 2005 Harvard Business Review work and expanded in "Competing Against Luck", 2016): people do not buy products or consume content for their features, they "hire" them to make progress on a functional, social, or emotional job. Applied here, every pillar and every call to action is mapped to a real job the audience is trying to get done (learn a skill, feel less alone, look credible to a boss, save time), which is why this skill insists each pillar connect to a core audience need before anything else. Source: Clayton M. Christensen et al., "Know Your Customers' Jobs to Be Done", Harvard Business Review, 2016.

Three audiences, same evidence: a **founder** uses pillars to build category authority, a **professional** uses them to be known for one thing inside a crowded org, and in **real life** they keep a passion project or community page coherent instead of random.

## 4. The decision rubric

This is the psychology layer. Before any content decision, run the conditions against the defaults. The override column wins when it applies.

| Condition the skill looks for | Default decision | Edge case that overrides |
|---|---|---|
| Idea does not map to any named pillar | Reject or reshape until it maps to one pillar | Timely cultural moment directly relevant to the audience: allow once, then propose it as a new pillar if it recurs |
| Idea maps to a pillar but serves no audience job (functional, social, emotional) | Reject: pillar fit alone is not enough | None. A pillar with no job behind it is a vanity topic |
| Weekly mix is drifting above 10% direct conversion | Hold promo, rebalance toward authority and trust content | Active launch window declared in business-context.md: temporarily allow a higher promo ratio for the stated window only |
| Strong idea, wrong platform for the format | Re-angle for the platform's native strength before publishing | None. Never copy-paste one piece across platforms |
| Same anchor already repurposed this week | Stagger the next derivative to a later day | Evergreen cornerstone worth a deliberate, spaced re-run |
| Call to action intensity does not match content type | Match intensity to the rubric in Output format (soft for value, strong for proof) | None |
| Member asks "what should I post" with no goal in view | Default every suggestion to the primary goal | A specific campaign goal stated for this period in working memory |
| Two ideas compete for one slot | Choose the one closer to the primary goal and the underserved pillar this week | A time-sensitive idea with a real deadline |

## 5. Workflow

1. Read inputs (Section 9) before deciding anything: the member's business context, the existing strategy, and any working-memory notes on current campaigns.
2. Confirm the primary goal. Every later choice is checked against it. Between this step and the next, note silently whether the goal has shifted since the strategy was last written; if it has, flag it rather than quietly working to an old goal.
3. Confirm or set the content pillars. Each pillar must connect to a named audience job. If a proposed pillar has no job behind it, drop it.
4. Map pillars to platforms using each platform's role, not by posting the same thing everywhere. Look at where this audience actually pays attention before assigning cadence.
5. Set the anchor-then-repurpose flow: one anchor piece feeds many platform-native derivatives.
6. Build the weekly rhythm from platform frequencies and the content mix ratio. Check the mix against the rubric (the 10% conversion ceiling) before locking it.
7. Attach calls to action by content type and intensity.
8. Pressure-test the week against the decision rubric, then log decisions and any proposed pillar changes.

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- **Always safe (act, then log):** draft the strategy or calendar, suggest pillars, re-angle an idea for a platform, rebalance the weekly mix toward the target ratio, flag an off-pillar idea.
- **Draft and wait for approval:** changing a content pillar, changing the primary goal, raising the direct-conversion ratio for a launch, adding or dropping a platform, publishing anything externally.
- **Never (no matter the tier):** publish externally without approval and a log entry, move money or commit to paid promotion, invent results, testimonials, or statistics for content, delete the strategy or calendar files, act outside the content remit.

## 7. Escalation

When unsure, route by urgency. Time-sensitive content decisions (a same-day cultural moment, a live launch question) go to the member in their fast channel. Strategic shifts (a new pillar, a changed primary goal, a new platform) go into the decision log flagged for review at the next planning check-in, not actioned silently. If inputs conflict (for example the strategy says one goal and working memory implies another), stop and ask rather than guess.

## 8. Responsible use

Tied to this skill's real failure modes: never fabricate client results, testimonials, statistics, or credentials to make a piece of content land. Never imply an outcome the member has not actually achieved. Never let a strong promo idea push the week past the agreed conversion ceiling and erode trust. When content or a calendar is produced with AI assistance and the member intends to publish it, keep that transparent in line with the member's disclosure preference. A human approves anything that goes out.

## 9. Inputs and memory

- **Reads:** `memory/business-context.md` (the member's business, audience, goal, voice, tools, and any overridable thresholds such as the launch-window conversion ratio); any working-memory files for the current period (for example a current-campaign note or a content backlog); `memory/industry-context.md` where the member uses one.
- **Writes:** `logs/activity-log.md` (what was drafted or rebalanced), `logs/decision-log.md` (pillar changes, goal changes, platform changes flagged for review), and the named strategy and calendar outputs the member keeps for this skill.

Never read "any relevant context". Read the named files above.

## The Anchor-Then-Repurpose Model

The member's content system follows a simple principle: create once, repurpose many times.

### The Flow

```
Anchor Content (Newsletter or long-form piece)
  |
  +-- LinkedIn post (thought leadership angle)
  +-- LinkedIn post (contrarian angle)
  +-- Instagram carousel (educational framework)
  +-- Instagram carousel (story version)
  +-- Instagram reel (key insight, 15-30s)
  +-- Instagram stories (behind-the-scenes)
  +-- Blog post (SEO-optimised expansion)
```

### Repurposing Rules

1. **Never copy-paste** across platforms. Adapt the message for each audience and format
2. **Change the angle:** one idea can be educational, personal, contrarian, or motivational
3. **Lead with the strongest hook** for each platform
4. **Adjust length and structure:** LinkedIn wants depth, Instagram wants visual, TikTok wants speed
5. **Stagger timing:** spread repurposed content across the week so it feels fresh

## Posting Rhythm

### Weekly Cadence

Use the platform frequencies above to build the weekly cadence. A balanced week alternates pillars and content types across the available slots.

### Content Mix (Weekly)

- **40%** Authority content: educational, frameworks, insights
- **25%** Trust content: stories, social proof, vulnerability
- **20%** Community content: engagement, questions, relatable moments
- **10%** Lead generation: lead magnet promotion, email list growth
- **5%** Direct conversion: offer promotion

This ratio ensures the audience always gets more value than asks, maintaining trust while driving commercial outcomes.

## 11. What good looks like

**Good example (annotated).**

> Pillar: "AI for time-poor founders". This week's anchor: a newsletter on "the three tasks every founder should automate first". [1] Derivatives: a LinkedIn thought-leadership post on the cost of doing those tasks by hand, a contrarian LinkedIn post ("automation is not about doing more, it is about doing less"), and an Instagram carousel of the three tasks. [2] Mix for the week sits at 40 authority / 25 trust / 20 community / 10 lead-gen / 5 conversion. [3] The carousel call to action is soft ("save this"), the lead-gen email is medium ("comment [your codeword] for the checklist"). [4]

1. Every piece maps to a named pillar and a real audience job (save time), per the governing principle and Jobs to Be Done.
2. One anchor, many platform-native angles, never a copy-paste.
3. The mix respects the conversion ceiling in the rubric.
4. Call to action intensity matches content type.

Across the three audiences this holds: a **founder** anchors on category authority, a **professional** anchors on the one thing they want to be known for at work, and in **real life** the anchor might be a community update for a local cause, repurposed the same way.

**Bad example (named failure mode: random acts of content).**

> Monday: a meme about Mondays. Tuesday: a hard sell for the course. Wednesday: a reshared article with no point of view. Thursday: another sell. Friday: a personal photo with no tie to any pillar.

Failure mode: no documented strategy, no pillars, no audience job, and a conversion ratio far above the ceiling. This is exactly the pattern the Content Marketing Institute research links to underperformance, and it trains the audience to ignore or distrust the feed. The fix is to route every one of those slots through the decision rubric first.
