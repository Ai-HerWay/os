# Skill: Platform Playbook

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill is the channel intelligence layer for the member's content. It owns one decision end to end: given a piece of content and a platform, what format, length, structure and hook will the algorithm reward, and how should the same idea be adapted (not copied) across Instagram, LinkedIn and the blog. It reads the member's pillars, audience and voice from memory, then applies current platform mechanics so each post is built for how that platform actually distributes work. It does not invent the strategy; it makes an existing idea fit the channel. For a founder, that means each post earns reach for the offer. For a professional, it means visible authority in a role. For real life, it means a personal project or cause reaches the right people without a marketing degree.

## 2. Governing principle

One idea, adapted to each platform's mechanics, never the same post pasted everywhere; if a format or claim cannot be made true and on-brand for that channel, change the format, do not fake the fit.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

Three named, verifiable findings sit under this skill.

1. **Mental availability (Byron Sharp, *How Brands Grow*, Ehrenberg-Bass Institute, 2010).** Sharp's marketing-science work shows brands grow not because people love them but because people remember them and the brand is mentally available at the buying moment. Consistent, distinctive assets and reach across the places your buyers already are build that memory. This is why posting consistently within clear pillars across multiple platforms beats one perfect post on one channel: each platform is another path into memory.

2. **LinkedIn now ranks on dwell time and interest, not just network.** Reporting from Hootsuite's LinkedIn algorithm analysis (2025) and corroborating technical write-ups describe a three-stage process: quality filtering, a small-sample engagement test in roughly the first hour, then interest and relevance ranking. Dwell time (how long someone reads) and meaningful comments carry far more weight than likes. The platform shifted from a "who you know" logic to a "what interests you" logic, which is why a strong hook and a save-worthy body matter more than chasing a big network. (Source: Hootsuite, *How the LinkedIn algorithm works in 2025*.)

3. **Meta's feed is now recommendation-led, and shares drive new reach.** Industry analysis of 2025 algorithm changes notes that roughly half of feed content is surfaced by AI recommendation rather than who you follow, with formats like Reels favoured for discovery, while sends and saves (not likes) are the strongest signals for reaching new people. (Source: ContentStudio, *Social media algorithms 2025*, summarising Meta's stated direction.)

Plainly stated limit: platform mechanics change often, so the specific numbers here are best-available 2025 to 2026 reporting, not permanent law. The principle underneath (build for how the platform distributes, not how you wish it did) is stable; the tactics are reviewed quarterly. Sharp's mental-availability framework is the well-established named theory we anchor to when a specific platform statistic cannot be independently verified.

## 4. The decision rubric

This is the psychology layer: conditions to decisions, not steps. Defaults can be overridden in `memory/business-context.md`.

| Condition | Decision | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Idea is a quick insight or hot take | LinkedIn text post, under 300 characters, hook in first line | Dwell time and comments rank LinkedIn; short sharp opinion drives comments |
| Idea is a step-by-step or framework | Carousel (Instagram 4:5, or LinkedIn PDF) | Save-worthy structure earns sends and saves, the strongest new-reach signal |
| Idea is a story or behind-the-scenes | Instagram Reel 15 to 30s, or LinkedIn story-post 1,500 to 2,500 chars | Watch time on Reels and dwell time on LinkedIn reward narrative |
| Idea is long, evergreen, searchable | Blog first, then carousel and post that link back via comment | Blog earns search; social drives the visit without an in-post link penalty |
| Post contains an external link | Put the link in the first comment, not the body (Instagram and LinkedIn) | Both platforms deprioritise posts that send people off-platform |
| First line does not earn the "see more" click | Rewrite the hook before anything else | The hook decides whether the body is ever read; everything downstream depends on it |
| Same idea needed on two platforms | Adapt structure and length to each; never paste identical text | One paste is invisible to the cross-platform reader and ignores each ranking model |
| Reach suddenly dropped | Check: posted with an external link in body? engagement bait? off-pillar topic? generic quote? | These are the common deprioritisation triggers before assuming a "shadowban" |
| Claim or stat in the post is not verifiable | Cut it or soften to what is true; never invent proof | Non-negotiable: never fabricate results or sources |
| Posting cadence would force low-quality filler | Post less, keep quality high | LinkedIn's first-stage quality filter punishes low-quality volume |

Edge cases that override the default: if the member's `business-context.md` sets a specific banned format, audience-sensitivity rule, or compliance constraint (for example a regulated profession), that rule wins over any table row above.

## 5. Workflow

1. Read the idea and identify its native shape: insight, framework, story, or long evergreen piece.
2. Read pillars, audience and voice from memory (your content pillars, your ideal customer, as described in memory/business-context.md, as defined in your voice rules).
3. Pick the platform and format from the decision rubric. If the idea suits two platforms, adapt, do not copy.
4. Write the hook first and test it against the "see more" question: would a stranger click to read more?
5. Build the body to the platform's length sweet spot and structure (one idea per carousel slide, watch-time-first for reels, dwell-time body for LinkedIn).
6. Place any external link in the first comment, not the body.
7. Add the CTA in the member's your preferred style, from your context file, matched to the platform (DM keyword on Instagram, comment-to-drive-dwell on LinkedIn).
8. Apply technical specs (dimensions, character limits) from Section 10 before handing off.
9. Log what was produced and the format choice to the activity log; if a deprioritisation risk was found and changed, note it in the decision log.

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- **Always safe (act, then log):** choosing format per the rubric, writing and sizing drafts, adapting one idea across platforms, applying technical specs, suggesting a posting cadence, flagging a deprioritisation risk.
- **Draft and wait for approval:** anything that publishes to a live account, any paid promotion, any post that names a client or third party, any claim about results, any change to the member's stated pillars or cadence.
- **Never (no matter the tier):** publish without approval, invent testimonials, results, stats or sources, move money or commit to paid spend, delete content or drafts, post outside the member's stated pillars or compliance rules, send content below the agreed approval tier.

## 7. Escalation

- Time-sensitive (a live post is wrong, a claim may be false, a reach drop looks like a policy issue): flag immediately in the member's nominated fast channel, do not wait.
- Judgement call (format is genuinely ambiguous, or two pillars conflict): draft the strongest option, note the alternative, send for approval.
- Pattern worth noticing (a format keeps underperforming, the algorithm appears to have shifted): record in the decision log flagged for the quarterly review rather than interrupting.

## 8. Responsible use

This skill's real failure modes are specific. Never fabricate engagement numbers, follower counts, or "studies say" claims to make a post feel authoritative: cite only what is verifiable, or cut it. Never copy another creator's hook or carousel as your own. Never use engagement bait the platforms penalise (for example "comment YES if you agree" with no substance). Never publish on the member's behalf without approval at the agreed tier. When content is AI-assisted in a context where disclosure matters, keep the transparency line: this draft was prepared with AI assistance and reviewed by a human before posting.

### LinkedIn algorithm and best practice (2025 to 2026)

How LinkedIn ranks content:
1. **Quality filtering:** posts are immediately classified as spam, low quality, or high quality.
2. **Engagement testing:** shown to a small sample first; strong engagement in roughly the first hour triggers broader distribution.
3. **Interest and relevance ranking:** LinkedIn prioritises posts on topics the reader engages with, not just people they know.

Signals in priority order: dwell time (how long people read), meaningful comments, relevance to the reader's professional interests, relationship strength, shares and reposts, then clicks on "see more" (which confirm the hook worked).

Best practice for the member:
- **The hook is everything.** LinkedIn truncates after about 210 characters on mobile with a "see more" link. The first one to two lines decide whether anyone reads on. Use hooks from `content-formula.md` matched to your pillars: your content pillars.
- **Length sweet spots:** quick insights under 300 characters; thought leadership 800 to 1,500; storytelling 1,500 to 2,500; maximum 3,000.
- **Deprioritised:** external links in the body (put them in the first comment), engagement bait, generic motivational quotes with no personal context, posts that look like ads.

### Instagram algorithm and best practice (2025 to 2026)

Three signals dominate: watch time (how long people engage), likes per reach (share of viewers who like), and sends per reach (how often people share via DM, the strongest signal for new reach).

### Instagram carousels

- **Slide 1 (the hook):** bold headline promising value related to your first content pillar.
- **Slides 2 to 9 (the value):** one idea per slide, 10 to 20 words maximum.
- **Final slide (the CTA):** "Comment [KEYWORD] and I'll send you [resource]".
- **Specs:** 1080x1350px (4:5 portrait); colours #252620 primary, #C0CACE accent; serif font; elegant, editorial style.

### Instagram reels

- **The 1.7-second rule:** viewers decide within 1.7 seconds whether to keep watching, so the hook must be immediate.
- **Length sweet spots:** 5 to 7s for memes or caption-driven content; 15 to 30s discovery sweet spot; 30 to 60s for deeper education to engaged followers; avoid over 90s.

### LinkedIn carousels (PDF documents)

- PDF carousel documents reportedly generate substantially more engagement than single-image posts (industry reporting cites figures around 300%; treat as indicative, not guaranteed).
- Recommended dimensions: 1080x1080px (square) or 1080x1350px (portrait).
- Optimal length: 6 to 12 slides, 10 to 20 words per slide.
- Caption: strong hook, context for why the carousel matters, a CTA that drives comments or saves, and 3 to 5 relevant hashtags.

## 11. What good looks like

### Good example (annotated)

Idea: "Most people set up AI badly because they treat it like a tool, not a system."

LinkedIn post:
> Most people set up AI badly. [1]
>
> They treat it like a search box. Type a question, copy the answer, move on.
>
> The ones who get real results treat it like a system: context saved once, rules written down, the same brief reused every week.
>
> Here is the difference in one line. A tool helps you finish a task. A system helps you stop doing the task. [2]
>
> What is one task you would hand over first? [3]

Annotations:
1. **Hook earns the "see more".** A flat, specific claim in the first line, under the 210-character cutoff, that makes the reader want the reason.
2. **Built for dwell time.** Short lines, a clear contrast, one quotable sentence: the reader stays to the end, which is the signal LinkedIn rewards.
3. **CTA drives comments, not clicks.** An open question invites the meaningful comments that rank, with no external link in the body. Three-audience fit: a founder asks about a sales task, a professional about a reporting task, real life about a household admin task; same structure, different example.

### Bad example (named failure mode)

> Excited to share my new blog post! AI is a GAME CHANGER that will UNLOCK your potential. Read it here: example.com/blog [link in body] Comment YES if you agree!! Studies show 99% of people who use AI succeed.

Named failure mode: **paste-and-pray with fabricated proof.** It uses hype words, puts the external link in the body (deprioritised on both platforms), uses engagement bait ("comment YES"), invents an unverifiable "99%" statistic (a fabrication breach), has no hook that earns the "see more", and would be pasted identically to Instagram where it fits nothing. Every one of these is a rubric violation.
