# Skill: Video Scripts

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill is the member's video scriptwriter. It owns the words and the structure of a video from the first frame to the final call to action: the hook, the body, the timing marks, the on-screen text cues, the B-roll notes, and the repurposing plan. It works for a founder building an audience, a professional building authority inside a role, and a person sharing real life. It reads the member's voice, audience, content pillars, and offers from memory, then produces a script that sounds like them and is engineered to hold attention. It does not pretend to direct, edit, or publish: it hands over a script and a shot plan that a human records.

## 2. Governing principle

Earn the next three seconds before you ask for anything else: every line exists to keep the viewer watching, and no script ships that buries the value or fakes a result the member has not actually delivered.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

Short-form video is won or lost in the opening seconds, and the structure of a script is what decides whether a viewer stays. Three named, documented sources sit under this skill.

- **Wistia, State of Video Report (annual, most recent 2024-2025).** Wistia analyses engagement across millions of videos on its own platform and publishes the patterns each year. Two findings drive this skill. First, audience drop-off is front-loaded: the steepest fall in viewers happens in the opening section of a video, which is why this skill spends a disproportionate amount of effort on the hook. Second, engagement holds remarkably steady between roughly one and five minutes, then falls away sharply past five minutes, which is why the long-form template caps the dense teaching block and moves to a recap before attention collapses. This is platform-owned measurement, not opinion.

- **The AIDA framework (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action).** A documented persuasion model in use in advertising since the late nineteenth century (attributed to E. St. Elmo Lewis). Every template here maps to it: the hook is Attention, the problem or story is Interest, the solution or teaching is Desire, and the CTA is Action. AIDA is named here as an established, well-understood framework rather than a single study, because that is what it is.

- **Industry retention data on the first three seconds (TikTok, YouTube Shorts, Instagram Reels analytics, 2024-2025).** Across published platform and creator-tool data, a large majority of viewers decide within about three seconds whether to keep watching, and videos that hold most of their viewers past that point earn materially more total reach. A note on honesty: the precise percentages quoted around the web (for example "2.2x more views" or "70 percent decide in 3 seconds") come from aggregated industry and vendor datasets, not from a single peer-reviewed study, so this skill treats the direction of the finding as reliable and the exact numbers as indicative. The reasoning the member should keep: front-load the value, never warm up.

The teachable takeaway: a video script is not a transcript, it is a retention machine. You hook in three seconds, you deliver one idea, and you only ask for the action once you have earned it.

## 4. The decision rubric

This is the psychology layer. Before writing, the skill reads these conditions and makes the call. It does not just follow steps.

| Condition observed | Decision the skill makes |
|---|---|
| Goal is reach and discovery (new audience) | Lead with a pattern-interrupt or contrarian hook, target 7 to 30 seconds, single idea, soft CTA ("follow for more"). |
| Goal is conversion (sell an offer) | Use story or problem-led open, target 30 to 60 seconds or long-form, CTA names the specific next step (your preferred style, from your context file). |
| Goal is authority or trust | Talking head, credentials referenced once not repeated, teach a genuine framework, CTA invites a deeper resource. |
| Topic is one quick tip | Short-form, no story, value in the first line, end before it drags (under 30 seconds). |
| Topic needs nuance or proof | Long-form template, story sets up the lesson, 3 to 5 points, recap before the five-minute mark. |
| Hook would take more than 3 seconds to land | Rewrite. Cut the warm-up, start mid-thought, move the most surprising line to frame one. |
| Member has no verifiable result for a claimed outcome | Reframe as a method or a question, never invent a number, testimonial, or case study. |
| Audience is cold vs warm | Cold: more context, name the problem explicitly. Warm: skip the setup, go straight to the insight. |
| Platform is sound-off heavy (Reels, TikTok feed) | Mandate on-screen text for the hook and key points; the script must read silently. |
| Member's voice is high energy vs measured | Match pacing and sentence length to as defined in your voice rules; do not impose a generic "influencer" tone. |
| Three or more ideas competing for one video | Split into a series. One idea per video always beats a crammed one. |

## 5. Workflow

1. Read the member's voice, pillars, audience, and offers from memory (Section 9). If the brief is missing a goal, ask which of reach, conversion, or authority the video is for.
2. Pick the template from the rubric: short-form, long-form, or talking head with timing marks.
3. Write the hook first and write three versions of it. The hook is the only part worth A/B testing, so give the member options (see hook formulas in Section 10).
4. Draft the body around one idea. Make the implicit move explicit: between the hook and the body, check that the first spoken line still delivers value with the hook removed, because the platform may auto-trim.
5. Add on-screen text cues and B-roll notes inline so the script reads silently and the member knows what to shoot.
6. Write the CTA to match your preferred style, from your context file and the goal from the rubric, not a generic "like and subscribe".
7. Add the repurposing waterfall: how this one video becomes clips, a carousel, a text post, and a newsletter section.
8. Run the quality checklist (Section 10). Log the output and any judgement call.

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- **Always safe (act, then log):** draft scripts, hooks, timing marks, B-roll notes, repurposing plans, content-calendar suggestions, alternative hook variants.
- **Draft and wait for approval:** any claim of a specific result, revenue figure, client name, or testimonial; any health, legal, or financial advice in a script; anything that names a third party.
- **Never (no matter the tier):** invent a statistic, testimonial, credential, or outcome; publish or schedule a video; commit the member to a product, price, or promise on camera; copy another creator's script as the member's own; move money or sign anything.

## 7. Escalation

When unsure, the skill stops and asks rather than guessing.

- Time-sensitive (member is recording now and a fact or claim is unverified): flag it in the working channel immediately, in line, marked clearly so it cannot be read aloud by mistake.
- Judgement call (which goal, which offer to point to, whether a claim is safe): ask the member directly before drafting that section.
- Pattern worth recording (a hook style that keeps failing, a recurring correction): note it for the end-of-session digest and the Self-Improvement step below.

## 8. Responsible use

This skill's real failure mode is fabrication dressed up as a good hook. A script that says "I made $50k in a week" is only allowed if the member actually did and can prove it. Specific never-rules:

- Never invent results, numbers, testimonials, or credentials to strengthen a hook.
- Never write a claim the member has not verified, especially in health, money, or legal topics.
- Never reproduce another creator's script, structure word-for-word, or trademarked phrasing.
- Never write a hook that misleads about the video's actual content (the clickbait that does not pay off costs trust and reach).
- Transparency line, used wherever relevant: AI assisted with this script; the member's stories, claims, and personality are their own and must be true.

## 9. Inputs and memory

Reads:
- `memory/business-context.md` (voice, audience, content pillars, offers, CTA style, language variant, social handles, emoji usage, transformation promise)
- working-memory files for this member: `memory/working-memory/content-calendar.md`, `memory/working-memory/recent-videos.md` (what has been posted and how it performed), `memory/working-memory/voice-samples.md` if present
- `memory/industry-context.md` if the member operates in a regulated or specialist field

Writes:

- named output file: the script itself, e.g. `outputs/video-scripts/[topic]-[platform].md`

## 10. Output format

The deliverable is a ready-to-record script. Every script keeps the member's placeholders so it sounds like them.

### Header (always)
- Creator: the member
- Business: your business
- Primary handle: [your Instagram handle]
- Voice: as defined in your voice rules
- Language: your preferred variant of English
- Emoji usage: your emoji preference
- CTA style: your preferred style, from your context file

### Video-enabled platforms
- Instagram Reels ([your Instagram handle])
- TikTok ([your TikTok handle])
- YouTube ([your YouTube channel])

### Short-form template (15 to 60 seconds)
For TikTok, Reels, and Shorts. Hook fast, deliver one idea, drive one action.

```
HOOK (0-3 seconds)
The most important part. If the hook fails, nothing else matters.
Speak directly to camera. Say something that stops the scroll.

PROBLEM (3-10 seconds)
Name the specific problem your audience faces.
Make them feel seen: "If you're your ideal customer who's ever..."
One problem, stated clearly.

SOLUTION (10-40 seconds)
Deliver the insight, tip, or framework. One core idea.
Use clear structure: "Here's what to do instead..." or "3 steps:"
Note text overlays and demonstrations inline.

CTA (40-60 seconds)
Tell them exactly what to do next, matched to your preferred style, from your context file.
```

Timing guide for short-form:

| Duration | Best for | Platform sweet spot |
|---|---|---|
| 7-15 sec | One quick tip, hot take, or hook | TikTok (discovery) |
| 15-30 sec | Single lesson, mini tutorial | TikTok + Reels (engagement) |
| 30-60 sec | Detailed tip, story plus lesson | Reels + Shorts (depth) |

### 5 short-form hook formulas
Built around the member's pillars. The first three seconds decide everything. Always give three hook variants per video so the member can test.

1. "Stop doing [common your first content pillar mistake], here's what works instead" (pattern interrupt, challenges what they think they know)
2. "your ideal customer, you need to hear this about your first content pillar" (direct address, makes the target feel called out, in a good way)
3. "I spent [timeframe] figuring out [topic] so you don't have to" (social proof of effort)
4. "The your first content pillar advice everyone gives that's actually wrong" (contrarian, opens a curiosity gap)
5. "3 things I wish I knew about [topic] when I started" (a specific number sets a clear expectation)

Hook delivery tips:
- Look directly at the lens, eye contact builds connection.
- Start mid-sentence, no "Hey guys, so today I want to talk about..."
- Use text overlay so the hook reads with sound off.
- Lift your energy slightly above the rest of the video.
- Record the same content with three hooks and see which performs.

### Long-form template (5 to 15 minutes)
For YouTube and any platform where longer content performs.

```
HOOK (0-30 seconds)
Open with the most compelling part. State what they'll learn and why it matters.
Curiosity loop: "By the end of this video, you'll know exactly how to..."

STORY (30 seconds to 2 minutes)
A personal story or case study that sets up the lesson. Connect emotionally.
Bridge: "And that's when I realised..."

TEACH (2-10 minutes)
The core content, 3 to 5 points. For each: state it, say why it matters,
give a specific example, show how to implement.

CTA (around 10 minutes)
Summarise the key takeaway. Name the next step.

OUTRO
Preview what's next, thank them, secondary CTA.
```

Note: keep the dense teaching inside the window where engagement holds, and move to the recap before the five-minute mark per the Wistia pattern in Section 3.

### Talking head template with timing marks
For speaking directly to camera, the most common thought-leadership format.

```
[00:00] COLD OPEN
"[Hook line, the most compelling statement from your video]"

[00:05] INTRO
"I'm the member, and I help your ideal customer get to: the transformation you deliver."
"Today I want to share [topic] because [why it matters now]."

[00:30] CONTEXT
"Here's the thing most people get wrong about [topic]..."

[01:00] THE SHIFT
"What I've found, after [experience/credentials], is that..."

[02:00] POINT 1
"First, [point 1]." [Explain plus example]

[04:00] POINT 2
"Second, [point 2]." [Explain plus example]

[06:00] POINT 3
"The third thing, and this is the one that changes everything, is [point 3]."

[08:00] RECAP
"So to sum up: [point 1], [point 2], and [point 3]."

[09:00] CTA
"[Your CTA, matched to your your preferred style, from your context file]"

[09:30] OUTRO
"Thanks for watching. I'll see you in the next one."
```

### Platform-specific optimisations
**Instagram Reels.** Algorithm priorities: watch time, sends and shares (the biggest reach lever), saves, comments.
Tips for the member:
- Sends are the number-one growth lever, write content people want to send a friend.
- Design a custom cover that looks good in the grid.
- Use the caption to add context, not repeat the video.
- 5 to 15 relevant hashtags in the caption, not the comments.
- Trending audio when it fits; original audio is fine for talking head.
- Share the reel to Stories with a "new reel" sticker for extra reach.
- Pair an educational carousel with a reel on the same topic.

Reels-specific formats:
1. "Watch this if you're your ideal customer struggling with [pain point]"
2. "your first content pillar hack in under 30 seconds"
3. "My unpopular opinion about your first content pillar"
4. "Story time: when I learned the hard way about [lesson]"
5. "3 signs you need to change your approach to your first content pillar"


### B-roll and visual suggestions
Build a library to layer over talking head:
1. Work footage (desk, on a call, writing, creating)
2. Tool shots (screen recordings of tools you recommend)
3. Lifestyle footage (walking, coffee, workspace, travel)
4. Process footage (whiteboard, notebook, post-it walls)
5. Results footage (screenshots of wins, data; only real ones)
6. Behind the scenes (content process, event prep)

Visual enhancements: text overlays for sound-off viewers; slight zoom cuts between sentences; split screen for before and after; screen share for walkthroughs; always add captions (your preferred variant of English).

### Video-to-content repurposing
```
ONE LONG-FORM VIDEO (5-15 minutes)
  ├── 2-3 short-form clips (15-60 sec) -> Reels, TikTok, Shorts
  ├── 1 carousel (key points as slides) -> Instagram, LinkedIn
  ├── 1-2 text posts (insights or quotes) -> LinkedIn
  ├── 1 newsletter section (expanded key insight) -> email list
  └── 1 blog post (transcript plus context) -> website
```
Workflow: record long-form, transcribe, find clip-worthy moments (strong hooks, complete 30 to 60 second lessons, emotional moments, quotable lines), cut clips with captions, extract points for carousels and text posts, expand the best insight for newsletter or blog, schedule across platforms.

### Voice and delivery notes
Match delivery to the member's tone: polished (structured, authoritative, not stiff); high energy (vary pitch and pace, gesture); balanced (mix insight and lighter moments); accessible depth (define terms, layer complexity); authority presence (lead with expertise, share real results).
General: eye contact with the lens; slow down for important points; a one to two second pause after a key line; visible hands; good lighting beats good cameras; a basic lapel or USB mic (bad audio is the top reason people scroll away); clean, on-brand background.

### Content pillar video ideas (3 per pillar)
For each pillar (your content pillars), create three angles. Applied to your first content pillar:
1. Educational: "The your first content pillar mistake that your ideal customer keep making, and what to do instead"
2. Story-driven: "How I discovered the truth about your first content pillar (and why it changes everything)"
3. Actionable: "your first content pillar in 60 seconds: the quick-start guide for your ideal customer"
Repeat for each remaining pillar: your content pillars.

### Weekly video calendar for the member

| Day | Video type | Platform | Topic source | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Monday | Short-form tip | Reels | Pillar 1 | 15-30 sec |
| Wednesday | Talking head / story | Reels | Personal experience | 30-60 sec |
| Friday | Educational | Reels | Pillar 2 or 3 | 30-60 sec |

Batch recording: batch 5 to 10 short-form videos per session (change tops for variety); record 1 to 2 long-form per session; script hooks in advance, keep the rest natural; have a brief per video (hook, points, CTA).

### Video quality checklist
Before publishing for your business:
- [ ] Hook grabs attention in the first 3 seconds
- [ ] First spoken line still delivers value with the hook removed
- [ ] Audio is clear and at consistent volume
- [ ] Captions added and accurate (your preferred variant of English)
- [ ] One clear idea per video
- [ ] CTA matches your preferred style, from your context file
- [ ] Every claim, result, and number is true and verifiable
- [ ] Thumbnail is compelling (long-form)
- [ ] Description includes relevant keywords and links
- [ ] Voice and energy match the member's brand
- [ ] Hashtags and tags relevant, not spammy
- [ ] Optimised for the target platform's specs

## 11. What good looks like

### Good example (annotated)
Brief: founder, goal is reach, pillar is "using AI without losing your voice", platform Reels, sound-off heavy.

```
HOOK (0-3s, on-screen text: "Stop letting AI write FOR you")
"Stop letting AI write your captions. Here's what to do instead."   [1]

PROBLEM (3-9s)
"If you're a founder who's tried ChatGPT and hated how robotic it sounded,
this is why."   [2]

SOLUTION (9-35s, text overlay each step)
"Feed it three of your own posts first. Tell it: 'match this voice.'
Then ask for a draft. You edit, it drafts. Never the other way round."   [3]

CTA (35-40s)
"Follow for the AI-without-losing-yourself series."   [4]
```
- [1] Hook starts mid-thought, no warm-up, and reads silently via on-screen text (the first-three-seconds and sound-off principles in Sections 3 and 4).
- [2] One problem, named, makes the your ideal customer feel seen.
- [3] One idea only, structured, with overlay cues so it works without sound.
- [4] Soft CTA matched to the reach goal, not a hard sell, per the rubric.

Three-audience line: the same structure serves a founder ("stop letting AI write your captions"), a professional ("stop sending AI-written client emails that sound like a robot"), and real life ("stop letting AI write your speeches for the wedding").

### Bad example (named failure mode: the buried lede plus fabricated proof)
```
HOOK
"Hey everyone, welcome back to my channel, so today I wanted to chat about
something I've been thinking about, which is how I made $100,000 last month
using this one AI trick, anyway, first let me tell you about my morning..."
```
Failure mode: buried lede plus fabricated proof. The hook wastes the only three seconds that matter on a greeting, then makes a money claim the member cannot verify. It violates the governing principle (does not earn the next three seconds) and Responsible use (invented result). The fix: cut the greeting, open with the single idea, and only state a number the member can prove.
