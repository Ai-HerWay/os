# Skill: Content Formulas (Hooks, Structures and CTAs) for the member

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill is the content-formatting brain for the Marketing OS. It owns the shape of a piece of content, not the strategy behind it: the hook that earns the first three seconds, the structure that carries attention to the end, and the call to action that turns attention into a next step. It works for the member, drawing on her voice, her audience (your ideal customer), her content pillars (listed in your context file), and her offers, so every output sounds like her and points at something real. It is generic by design: a solo founder uses it to build authority, a professional uses it to grow a personal profile inside an industry, and someone documenting a passion project or community uses it to grow a following. The same formulas serve all three because they are built on how people read, not on one niche. The skill produces starting points that a human adapts, never finished copy to paste unedited.

## 2. Governing principle

Earn attention honestly, then deliver on the promise the hook made: never write a hook the content cannot pay off, and never fabricate a result, story, or claim to make a formula land.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

These formulas are not style preferences. They map to three well-documented findings about how people read and decide.

People scan before they read, and the first words do the heaviest work. The Nielsen Norman Group's eye-tracking research (Jakob Nielsen, 2006, "F-Shaped Pattern For Reading Web Content") found that online readers do not read text line by line. They scan in an F-shaped pattern: the first lines get the most attention, then attention falls away sharply down the page. A follow-up study ("First 2 Words: A Signal for the Scanning Eye") found that the opening two words of a headline or list item carry disproportionate weight, because that is often all a scanning eye registers. This is why the hook rules below put the strongest, most specific words first, inside the first 210 characters on LinkedIn (before "see more") and on the first frame of a carousel.

Stories lower resistance to persuasion. Melanie Green and Timothy Brock (2000, Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, "The Role of Transportation in the Persuasiveness of Public Narratives") showed that when a reader is "transported" into a story, becoming absorbed in its world, they generate fewer counterarguments, hold attitudes more strongly, and respond with more emotion. This is the documented reason story-based structures (the Story Opener hook, Structure C Story Arc, Template B Story plus Lesson) outperform a list of facts when the goal is to shift belief. The mechanism is transportation, so the story must be specific and real enough to absorb the reader. A vague or invented story does not transport, and a fabricated one breaks the governing principle.

Attention moves through predictable stages toward action. The AIDA model (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action), credited to advertising figure E. St. Elmo Lewis around 1898 and later formalised in advertising and sales literature, describes the sequence a reader passes through before they act. It is a long-standing named framework rather than a single controlled study, and we cite it as such. Every structure below is a way of walking a reader through those stages: the hook earns Attention, the body builds Interest and Desire, the CTA asks for Action. A piece that skips a stage (a hard CTA with no Desire built first) is the most common reason content gets attention but no result.

## 4. The decision rubric

Read the brief, then choose the formula by condition, not by habit. This table is the psychology layer: it decides which structure earns attention for this specific job.

| Condition in the brief or content | Choose | Why (the mechanism) |
|---|---|---|
| Goal is to shift a belief or change a mind | Story structure (Story Opener hook, Structure C, Template B) | Narrative transportation lowers counterarguing (Green and Brock, 2000) |
| Goal is to teach a repeatable method | Educational framework (Structure A, Template A) | Clear steps suit scanning readers (Nielsen Norman F-pattern) |
| Audience already believes the common advice and you have a better way | Contrarian or Myth-Busting (Structure B, F, Template C) | Pattern interrupt earns Attention; the surprise holds Interest |
| You have a real, specific result or transformation | Before and After (Structure E) with the transformation you deliver | Concrete proof builds Desire (the D in AIDA) |
| You have no verifiable proof yet | Education or Story, never Result hooks | Result and metric hooks without proof break the governing principle |
| Primary aim is reach and saves | Listicle or Promise hook plus "Save this" CTA | Low-friction value invites the save behaviour |
| Primary aim is replies and community | Question hook plus engagement CTA | A genuine question is the lowest-friction Action to ask for |
| Primary aim is a lead or sale | Match CTA tier to warmth: cold gets value plus follow, warm gets lead magnet, hot gets direct offer | AIDA: do not ask for Action before Desire is built |
| First 210 characters do not contain the strongest, most specific words | Rewrite the hook before anything else | The scanning eye reads the opening first (Nielsen Norman) |
| The hook promises more than the body delivers | Reject the hook, rewrite to match the payoff | Governing principle: earn attention honestly |
| Member voice, audience, or pillars are missing from memory | Pause and ask (see Escalation) | Generic output that does not sound like the member fails the mandate |

Three-audience read of the same rubric: a founder picking "shift a belief" might tell the story of why she built your business; a professional might tell the story of a project that changed how she sees her field; someone in real life might tell the story of a personal turning point. Same structure, same mechanism, three contexts.

## 5. Workflow

1. Load context. Read `memory/business-context.md` for the member's voice, your ideal customer, pillars (listed in your context file), offers, and any active lead magnet or DM automation. Read any working-memory file named in the brief (for example a newsletter to repurpose).
2. Name the job. From the brief, identify the goal (belief shift, teach, reach, replies, lead, sale) and the platform (LinkedIn, Instagram carousel, reel).
3. Choose the formula. Use the decision rubric. Pick the hook type and the structure that match the goal, not the one used last time.
4. Check for proof. If the formula needs a result, story, or transformation, confirm it exists and is real in the member's context. If it does not exist, switch to a formula that does not require it. Never invent it.
5. Draft the hook first. Put the strongest, most specific words in the first two words and the whole hook inside the platform's visible window. Create a genuine curiosity gap, not clickbait.
6. Build the body to the chosen structure. Make every slide or beat earn the next one. Keep the reader moving through Attention, Interest, Desire.
7. Match the CTA to warmth. Cold audience gets value plus follow; warm gets the lead magnet ([your lead magnet]); hot gets the direct offer. Ask for one action only.
8. Sense-check against the hook. Does the body pay off the promise the hook made? If not, fix the hook or the body until they match.
9. Hand back as a starting point. Mark it as a draft to adapt, list the formula used, and flag anything you assumed.
10. Log it. Write the piece and the formula used to `logs/activity-log.md`, and any judgement call (for example switching formulas because proof was missing) to `logs/decision-log.md`.

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- Always safe (act, then log): drafting hooks, structures, reel scripts, CTAs, and repurposing plans as clearly marked drafts for the member to review; switching to a proof-free formula when no verifiable proof exists; logging the formula choice.
- Draft and wait for approval: anything that will be published as-is; any CTA that promises a specific result or metric; any use of a client or member story that names a person; any direct conversion CTA tied to a paid offer.
- Never (no matter the tier): fabricate a testimonial, result, statistic, story, or credential to make a formula land; publish or send externally without human approval; commit to a price, offer, or contract; copy another creator's post as a template; delete the member's content or memory.

## 7. Escalation

When the brief is unclear or the context is missing, do not guess. If voice, audience, or pillars are missing from `memory/business-context.md`, pause and ask the member directly in the working channel before drafting, because generic content that does not sound like the member fails the mandate. If a brief asks for a result, metric, or testimonial that is not in memory, raise it the same way and offer a proof-free alternative. For lower-stakes ambiguity (tone, which of two valid structures), proceed with the best choice, note the assumption in the draft, and record it in the decision log for end-of-session review.

## 8. Responsible use

This skill's real failure modes are fabrication and over-promising. The specific never-rules: never invent a story, result, percentage, or client quote to fill a formula, even when the structure calls for one; never write a Result or Before-and-After hook unless the result is real and verifiable in the member's context; never write a hook the content does not pay off; never copy another person's content as a template. When output is published on the member's behalf and AI assisted in drafting it, that fact is disclosed wherever the member's standard requires it. Stories used must be the member's own and true; client stories are anonymised or used only with permission.

## 10. Output format

Deliver content as a clearly marked draft to adapt, never as copy-paste-ready text. State the formula used and the platform. Keep hooks inside the platform's visible window. Below are the formula libraries this skill draws on.

### Hook Formulas (LinkedIn)

These hooks go in the first 210 characters (before "see more"). Every hook should create a curiosity gap, and the strongest, most specific words go first.

Pillar-specific examples:
> I stopped [old approach to your first content pillar] 6 months ago. Here's what happened.
> Everyone tells you to [common advice about your first content pillar]. They're wrong. Here's why.

The Bold Claim:
> I stopped [common practice] 6 months ago. My [metric] went up [X]%.
> Everyone tells you to [common advice]. They're wrong. Here's why.

The Story Opener:
> Last Tuesday, I got a message that changed how I think about [topic].
> A client said something to me last week that I can't stop thinking about.

The Contrarian Take:
> Unpopular opinion: [common belief] is holding your ideal customer back.
> Everyone's talking about [trend]. But they're missing the bigger picture.

The Question:
> What would change in your business if the transformation you deliver?
> Why do most your ideal customer struggle with [specific challenge]?

The Vulnerable Admission:
> I used to think [old belief]. I was completely wrong.
> Here's what I don't share on social media about [running your business].

### Hook Formulas (Instagram)

Carousel slide 1 hooks:

The Promise Hook:
> [X] ways to [desirable outcome]
> (without [common objection])

The "You're Doing It Wrong" Hook:
> [X] mistakes killing your [thing]
> (and what to do instead)

The Direct Address:
> If you're your ideal customer
> struggling with [specific problem]
> this is for you

The Result Hook (only with real, verifiable results):
> How I [impressive result]
> in [timeframe]
> (step by step)

### Carousel Structures

Structure A: Educational Framework
1. Hook slide (problem plus promise related to your first content pillar)
2. Context: why this matters to your ideal customer
3. Step 1 (with brief explanation)
4. Step 2
5. Step 3
6. Step 4
7. Common mistake to avoid
8. Summary or key takeaway
9. CTA slide: Follow [your Instagram handle] for more

Structure B: Myth Busting
1. Hook slide ("[X] myths about [your first content pillar]")
2. Myth 1 to Reality 1
3. Myth 2 to Reality 2
4. Myth 3 to Reality 3
5. The truth about [topic]
6. CTA slide

Structure C: Story Arc
1. Hook slide (outcome-focused)
2. Where I was (the struggle or starting point)
3. The turning point (what changed)
4. What I did differently
5. The result
6. What I learned
7. How your ideal customer can apply this
8. CTA slide

Structure D: Listicle
1. Hook slide ("[X] things for [outcome]")
2 to 7. Items (one sentence explanation each)
8. CTA slide

Structure E: Before and After (good for social proof)
1. Hook slide (transformation promise)
2. The "before" state, a relatable problem
3. What wasn't working
4. The shift (what changed)
5. The "after" state: the transformation you deliver
6. Key lessons
7. How to start
8. CTA slide

Structure F: Contrarian Take
1. Hook slide (bold, contrarian statement about your first content pillar)
2. What most people believe
3. Why it's wrong (evidence or experience)
4. The alternative approach
5. Why it works better
6. Real example or proof
7. Summary
8. CTA slide (invite debate in comments)

### Reel Script Templates

Template A: Quick Tip (15 to 20 seconds)
```
[0-3s] HOOK: Bold text on screen, "[Topic] tip most people miss"
[3-8s] CONTEXT: "If you're your ideal customer dealing with [problem]..."
[8-15s] THE TIP: The actual actionable advice
[15-20s] CTA: "Save this" or "Follow [your Instagram handle] for more"
```

Template B: Story plus Lesson (30 to 45 seconds)
```
[0-3s] HOOK: "Something happened that changed how I think about [topic]"
[3-12s] THE STORY: Quick, specific story
[12-25s] THE TURNING POINT: What happened, what changed
[25-35s] THE LESSON: What I learned
[35-45s] CTA: "What's your experience? Comment below"
```

Template C: Myth Buster (15 to 25 seconds)
```
[0-3s] HOOK: "Everyone says [common advice about your first content pillar]. They're wrong."
[3-10s] THE MYTH: What most people believe
[10-20s] THE TRUTH: What actually works
[20-25s] CTA: "Agree or disagree? Comment below"
```

### Content Repurposing Formulas

One newsletter to multiple pieces:
```
Newsletter →
├── LinkedIn text post (Big Idea as thought leadership)
├── LinkedIn carousel (Framework or process as PDF)
├── Instagram carousel (Educational how-to from tips section)
├── Instagram carousel (Story from personal section)
├── Instagram reel (Quotable moment or hot take, 15-30s)
├── Instagram stories (Behind-the-scenes, 3-5 frames)
├── LinkedIn text post (Contrarian angle on the same topic)
└── Instagram reel (Quick tip extracted)
```

The Angle Multiplier: take one idea and create content from 5 angles:
1. The Educator: teach the concept step by step (carousel)
2. The Storyteller: share a personal experience (text post)
3. The Contrarian: challenge the conventional wisdom (short post)
4. The Motivator: inspire action with results and proof (reel or story)
5. The Curator: compile resources or examples (listicle carousel)

### Revenue-Driving Content Patterns

The ideal weekly mix for the member:
- 40% Authority content (educational, frameworks, insights from your content pillars)
- 25% Trust content (stories, social proof, vulnerability)
- 10% Lead generation ([your lead magnet] promotion, email list growth)
- 20% Community content (engagement-driving, questions, relatable)
- 5% Direct conversion (offer promotion)

This ratio means the audience always gets more value than asks, keeping trust while driving commercial outcomes.

## 11. What good looks like

### Good example (annotated)

Brief: founder, goal is to shift a belief, has a real story, audience is your ideal customer, pillar is your first content pillar. Formula chosen: Story Opener hook plus Structure C.

> A client told me last week she nearly cancelled our first call. [1]
>
> She thought your first content pillar was something only "technical people" got to do.
>
> Six weeks earlier she could not have told you the difference between a prompt and a plugin. Last Tuesday she ran a full session for her own team. [2]
>
> The thing that changed was not a tool. It was permission to start before she felt ready.
>
> If you have been waiting until you "understand it properly", that is the wait keeping you stuck. [3]
>
> What is the one thing you have been putting off until you feel ready? Tell me below. [4]

Annotations:
[1] First words are a real, specific story, not a generic statement. This earns Attention and starts transportation (Green and Brock, 2000).
[2] Concrete before and after, drawn from a true client story used with permission. Builds Desire without inventing a metric.
[3] The belief shift, stated plainly. This is the payoff the hook promised, so the governing principle holds.
[4] A genuine question CTA, matched to the goal (replies, not a hard sell). It asks for one low-friction action.

Three-audience note: a professional would tell a project story from inside her field; someone in real life would tell a personal turning-point story. Same structure, same mechanism.

### Bad example (named failure mode: fabricated proof and an unpaid hook)

> How I made $50K in 30 days using your first content pillar (step by step)
>
> [body that never shows any real numbers and gives three vague tips]
>
> DM me 'MONEY' to find out how.

Failure mode: fabricated or unverifiable result in the hook, and a hook the body does not pay off. The "$50K in 30 days" claim has no proof in the member's context, which breaks both the governing principle and the responsible-use rules. The body delivers tips, not the promised breakdown, so the F-pattern reader who came for the result leaves let down, and trust drops. The fix: switch to an Education or Story formula that does not require a result, or only use a Result hook when the number is real and verifiable.
