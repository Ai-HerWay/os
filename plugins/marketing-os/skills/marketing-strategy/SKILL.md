# Skill: Marketing Strategy

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill owns the marketing strategy for your business: the layer that decides what to do before any post, campaign, or ad is made. It works for the person whose growth it governs (a founder building a market, a professional building a reputation inside an industry, or someone in real life building support for a cause). It owns the full chain from positioning to primary objective to channel choice to the funnel that moves a stranger to [your primary goal]. It decides where to compete, where to refuse to compete, and how the pieces connect into one coherent plan. It does not write the content or run the campaigns (other skills do that). It decides what those pieces are for and why they are worth doing.

## 2. Governing principle

Strategy is a set of deliberate choices about where to play and where not to: never spread effort across every channel and message at once, and never approve a tactic that does not serve the one named primary objective.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

Three established, named bodies of work underpin this skill.

**Positioning is the frame that makes everything else land.** April Dunford, in "Obviously Awesome" (2019), drawn from her work positioning over 200 companies, defines positioning as the context you set so a buyer instantly understands what you are and why you matter. Her method runs in order: competitive alternatives (including "do nothing" and "hire someone"), then the unique attributes only you have, then the value those attributes create, then the customers who care most, then the market category you frame yourself within. Get the order wrong and the differentiation collapses. This is the named framework behind the Positioning section below, and the reason this skill defines positioning before it picks a single channel. Source: April Dunford, "Obviously Awesome: How to Nail Product Positioning so Customers Get It, Buy It, Love It", 2019.

**Brands grow by being easy to think of and easy to buy.** Byron Sharp and the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute for Marketing Science, in "How Brands Grow" (2010), set out two evidenced drivers of growth: mental availability (how readily the brand comes to mind in a buying moment, through memory structures linked to real buying occasions) and physical availability (how easy the brand is to actually find and buy). Their evidence shows growth comes mainly from reaching more category buyers more often, not from squeezing existing ones. Applied here, the funnel and channel choices are built to grow mental availability (be remembered at the moment of need) and physical availability (be findable and frictionless to act on), which is why the strategy invests in reach and ease, not just persuasion. Source: Byron Sharp, "How Brands Grow: What Marketers Don't Know", Ehrenberg-Bass Institute, 2010.

**Strategy is choosing what not to do.** The discipline of refusing channels and messages that do not serve the objective is the core of competitive strategy as articulated by Michael Porter, who argued that the essence of strategy is choosing a distinctive set of activities and deliberately deciding what not to do, because trying to be everything to everyone leaves a business stuck in the middle and beaten by focused rivals. We cite this as the established strategy framework rather than a single controlled study, and it is why this skill's rubric actively names channels to ignore, not only channels to invest in. Source: Michael E. Porter, "What Is Strategy?", Harvard Business Review, 1996.

Three audiences, same evidence: a **founder** uses positioning to carve a category she can own, a **professional** uses it to be the obvious choice for one kind of work inside her field, and in **real life** it keeps a cause clearly understood and easy to support instead of vague.

## 4. The decision rubric

This is the psychology layer. Before any strategic call, run the conditions against the defaults. The override column wins when it applies.

| Condition the skill looks for | Default decision | Edge case that overrides |
|---|---|---|
| No clear positioning (the member cannot say what they are an alternative to) | Stop. Define positioning in Dunford's order before any channel or funnel work | None. Channel spend on top of weak positioning wastes the spend |
| More than one primary objective is named | Force a single primary objective; demote the rest to secondary | A declared launch window in business-context.md may set a different primary objective for that window only |
| Effort is spread across four or more channels with none owned | Cut to one or two channels where the audience already pays attention; pause the rest | A genuinely tested channel already converting stays, even if it is the third |
| A channel is "industry default" but the audience is not there | Ignore it and say so explicitly | Distribution channel the buyer literally uses to find and buy (physical availability) stays in |
| A tactic is proposed with no link to the primary objective | Reject it, no matter how on-trend | A low-cost brand-awareness play that builds mental availability for the stated audience |
| Funnel has a strong top and no defined path to [your primary goal] | Build the missing middle and bottom before adding more awareness | A deliberate pure-awareness phase declared for a new market |
| Member wants to copy a competitor's channel mix | Re-derive the mix from this audience and positioning, not the competitor | The competitor is genuinely fishing in the same pond and it is tested, not assumed |
| Two channels compete for limited budget | Fund the one closer to where the audience already is and the primary objective | A time-sensitive distribution opportunity with a real deadline |

## 5. Workflow

1. Read inputs (Section 9) before deciding anything: the member's business context, any existing positioning or strategy, and working-memory notes on current campaigns or markets.
2. Define or confirm positioning in Dunford's order: competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value, best-fit customers, market category. The implicit move here: name what the audience would do instead of choosing the member ("do nothing", "hire someone", "use a spreadsheet") before claiming any differentiation.
3. Set the single primary objective. Every later choice is checked against it. If two objectives compete, force a choice and demote the rest.
4. Map the funnel from awareness to [your primary goal]: how a stranger becomes aware, interested, trusting, and ready to act. Note silently where the funnel currently leaks before adding anything new.
5. Choose channels by where this audience already is and what builds mental and physical availability, not by industry default. Name the channels to invest in and, explicitly, the channels to ignore.
6. Connect the pieces: each channel has a job in the funnel, and each funnel stage has a channel and a next step. No orphan tactics.
7. Pressure-test the whole plan against the decision rubric, then log the strategy and any positioning or objective changes.

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- **Always safe (act, then log):** draft the strategy, positioning, funnel map, or channel plan; recommend channels to cut; flag a tactic that does not serve the objective; map where the funnel leaks.
- **Draft and wait for approval:** changing the positioning, changing the primary objective, adding or dropping a channel the member actively uses, committing to paid channels or budget.
- **Never (no matter the tier):** commit budget or move money, sign or imply a partnership, invent market data, competitor claims, or results, delete the strategy files, act outside the marketing remit.

## 7. Escalation

When unsure, route by stakes. Time-sensitive opportunities (a distribution deal with a deadline, a fast-moving market shift) go to the member in their fast channel. Structural changes (new positioning, a changed primary objective, a new paid channel) go into the decision log flagged for review at the next planning check-in, not actioned silently. If inputs conflict (the strategy implies one objective and working memory another), stop and ask rather than guess. If positioning cannot be derived because the member cannot name their competitive alternatives, escalate before building anything downstream.

## 8. Responsible use

Tied to this skill's real failure modes: never fabricate market size, competitor behaviour, channel results, or audience data to justify a strategy. Never claim a position the member cannot actually defend (do not say "the only", "the first", or "the leading" unless it is true and evidenced). Never commit budget or paid channels without explicit human approval. When a strategy is produced with AI assistance and the member acts on it, keep that transparent in line with the member's disclosure preference. A human approves any change to positioning, objective, or budget.

## 9. Inputs and memory

- **Reads:** `memory/business-context.md` (the member's business, audience, offer, primary goal, voice, current channels, budget posture, and any overridable thresholds such as a declared launch window); any working-memory files for the current period (a current-campaign note, a market or competitor note); `memory/industry-context.md` where the member uses one.
- **Writes:** `logs/activity-log.md` (what was drafted or recommended), `logs/decision-log.md` (positioning changes, objective changes, channel changes flagged for review), and the named strategy and funnel-map outputs the member keeps for this skill.

Never read "any relevant context". Read the named files above.

## 10. Output format

The deliverable is the marketing strategy document below. Keep this structure, the section order, and every placeholder.

---

# Marketing Strategy: your business

> Generated for the member. This is the plan above all content and campaigns: where we compete, where we do not, and how a stranger becomes [your primary goal].

---

## Positioning

How the member is understood and why the right person chooses your business over the alternatives. Built in order.

1. **Competitive alternatives:** what your ideal customer would do instead (often "do nothing", a manual workaround, or a generalist).
2. **Unique attributes:** what your business has that the alternatives do not.
3. **Value:** the concrete outcome those attributes create for your ideal customer.
4. **Best-fit audience:** your ideal customer, as described in memory/business-context.md, the people who care most about that value.
5. **Market category:** the frame the member sits in so the value is obvious at a glance.

**Positioning statement:** [your positioning statement]

---

## Primary Objective

[your primary goal]

This is the one objective. Secondary goals support it; they never compete with it. Every channel and tactic below is checked against this objective before it ships.

---

## The Funnel (awareness to [your primary goal])

How a stranger becomes a customer, member, or supporter.

| Stage | Audience state | Job of marketing here | Primary channel |
|---|---|---|---|
| Awareness | Does not know your business exists | Be seen by the right your ideal customer; build mental availability | [your awareness channel] |
| Interest | Knows, not yet convinced | Teach and demonstrate value; earn attention | [your interest-building channel] |
| Trust | Convinced of value, not yet acting | Proof, stories, and a low-risk next step | [your highest-trust channel] |
| Action | Ready to move | One clear path to [your primary goal], easy to act on (physical availability) | [your action channel] |

Each stage must have a defined next step into the stage below. A funnel with a strong top and no middle leaks.

---

## Channel Strategy

### Invest in

These are where your ideal customer already pays attention and where your business can build presence over time.

[the channels you invest in]

### Deliberately ignore

Saying no is the strategy. These channels are skipped on purpose, with the reason, so effort is not spread thin.

[the channels you deliberately ignore]

**Why ignore matters:** focus beats presence everywhere. One or two owned channels outperform five neglected ones.

---

## How The Pieces Fit

- Positioning sets the message every channel carries.
- The primary objective is the single test every tactic passes or fails.
- Each funnel stage has a channel and a next step.
- Content and campaigns (other skills) execute inside this plan; they do not invent their own direction.

When in doubt about any marketing decision, ask: "Does this serve the primary objective, and is it on a channel we have chosen to invest in?"

## 11. What good looks like

**Good example (annotated).**

> Positioning: "For time-poor founders who would otherwise muddle through AI alone [1], your business is the structured AI operating system, not another course." Primary objective: 50 Hub members this quarter. [2] Funnel: awareness on LinkedIn (founders are there), interest via the newsletter, trust via member stories, action via one Hub page. [3] Invest in: LinkedIn and the newsletter. Deliberately ignore: TikTok and X, because this audience is not there and the team cannot sustain both. [4]

1. Positioning names the real alternative ("muddle through alone") before the differentiation, per Dunford's order.
2. One primary objective, not three.
3. Every funnel stage has a channel and a next step into the stage below, building mental availability (be remembered) and physical availability (one easy action page).
4. The strategy says no out loud, with a reason, per Porter.

Across the three audiences this holds: a **founder** positions to own a category, a **professional** positions to be the obvious choice for one kind of work in her field, and in **real life** the same structure positions a community cause so it is easy to understand and support.

**Bad example (named failure mode: stuck in the middle).**

> "We will be everywhere: Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn, YouTube, a podcast, a blog, and ads. Our goal is more awareness, more leads, more sales, and more community. We are like our competitor but better."

Failure mode: no positioning (only "better than them"), four competing objectives, and effort spread across seven channels with none owned. This is exactly the "stuck in the middle" pattern Porter warns produces no advantage, and it leaves the funnel with a noisy top and no path to action. The fix is to define positioning in order, force one primary objective, and cut to the one or two channels where the audience actually is.
