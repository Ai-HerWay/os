# Skill: DM Conversation Scripts for your business

> Generated for the member. These scripts are starting points, always personalise to the individual conversation. Sound like a human, not a bot.

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill owns one-to-one conversations in the direct-message channel from first reply to the moment an offer is made or the conversation is gracefully closed. It works for whoever runs the inbox: the founder doing it herself, a team member or virtual assistant handling DMs on the founder's behalf, or a professional managing a personal brand alongside a day job. It covers codeword openers, lead-magnet delivery, qualification, soft and direct closes, objection responses, follow-up sequences, and cold outreach. It does not own paid ads, broadcast email, or public comment threads. The jump it supports is L1 (you write each DM by hand) to L3 (a personal DM assistant that drafts in your voice and you approve), with optional L2 automation for the first codeword reply only.

Three audiences, same skill: a founder uses it to convert [your Instagram handle] followers into [your primary revenue path]; a professional uses it to answer LinkedIn enquiries and book calls without sounding scripted; in real life the same patterns help with any one-to-one ask (a community group reply, a school fundraising message, a polite decline) where warmth and a clear next step matter.

## 2. Governing principle

Earn the next message before you send it: every DM must be wanted, relevant, and personal to the person reading it, and the moment someone signals no, you stop with grace.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

Three named, established ideas sit under this skill.

First, **permission marketing** (Seth Godin, *Permission Marketing: Turning Strangers into Friends and Friends into Customers*, 1999). Godin defines permission marketing as the privilege of delivering anticipated, personal, and relevant messages to people who actually want them, and he draws a hard line between real permission and presumed permission: getting hold of someone's handle is not consent to pitch them. The codeword opener, the lead-magnet reply, and the cold-DM warm-up in this skill all exist to earn that permission first. Source: [Seth Godin, Permission Marketing (1999)](https://seths.blog/2008/01/permission-mark/).

Second, **conversational marketing** as a named category (David Cancel and Dave Gerhardt, *Conversational Marketing*, Drift, 2019). Drift coined the term for a method built on real-time, one-to-one conversations using the channels people already use in their personal lives (chat, text, DM) rather than forms and one-way email. The shift from broadcast to conversation is the whole reason a DM strategy outperforms a generic funnel: you respond to a real person in real time. Source: [David Cancel and Dave Gerhardt, Conversational Marketing (Drift, 2019)](https://www.drift.com/conversational-marketing-book/).

Third, the **response-rate evidence** for direct channels over broadcast email. Reported benchmarks consistently show direct, conversational channels out-responding email by a wide margin: LinkedIn message reply rates commonly fall in the 15 to 25 per cent range versus low single digits for cold email, and personalising an outreach message lifts response materially over a generic send. Plain caveat: these are industry-reported benchmarks (for example the ANA/DMA Response Rate Report and vendor studies), not a single peer-reviewed trial, and your own numbers will vary by audience and offer. Treat them as direction, not promises. Source: [SMS vs Email Marketing data study, Kixie](https://www.kixie.com/sales-blog/sms-vs-email-marketing-a-data-driven-study-for-your-sales-strategy/).

The teaching point: a DM works because it is anticipated, personal, relevant, and two-way. Strip any one of those and you are back to interruption, and interruption is what people mute.

## 4. The decision rubric

This is the psychology layer. Read the conversation, then choose the move. Defaults can be overridden by your rules in `memory/business-context.md`.

| What you observe in the conversation | What it usually means | The move to make |
|---|---|---|
| They used a codeword or downloaded a lead magnet | Warm, opted in, low friction | Deliver immediately, then ask one open question. Do not pitch yet. |
| They asked a specific question about an offer | Buying signal, mid-funnel | Answer plainly, then offer the soft close. Match to [your primary revenue path]. |
| They shared a problem but not a goal | Aware, not yet committed | Ask the goal question (magic-wand) before any offer. |
| Replies are short, slow, or one word | Low energy or busy, not rejection | Slow down, add value, do not chase. One light touch, then wait. |
| "I don't have time / it's too expensive / I need to think" | Hesitation, not no | Validate first, reframe once, then leave the door open. Never push twice. |
| They go quiet after interest | Life got busy or soft no | Follow-up sequence, value-led, maximum three touches total. |
| Cold contact, no prior engagement | No permission yet | Warm up first (engage their content 1 to 2 weeks), lead with value, never open with a pitch. |
| "No thanks / not interested / please stop" | A clear no | Acknowledge warmly, thank them, stop. Do not re-enter the sequence. |
| Message involves money owed, refunds, a complaint, legal, or distress | Out of DM-script scope | Escalate (see section 7). Do not improvise a commitment. |
| They are under 18 or it is ambiguous | Safeguarding risk | Do not qualify or pitch. Keep it general and escalate. |

Edge case that overrides every default: if continuing would feel like pressure to the person reading it, stop. A relationship kept warm beats a sale forced cold.

## 5. Workflow

1. **Read before you type.** Open their profile and the thread. Note one specific, real detail (a recent post, the comment they left, the magnet they grabbed). Generic openers are the fastest way to sound like a bot.
2. **Classify the conversation** using the rubric in section 4. Decide the move before you draft.
3. **Deliver value or answer the question first.** If they downloaded something, send it. If they asked something, answer it. Earn the next message.
4. **Ask one open question**, not four. Space qualification across the conversation. Listen more than you pitch.
5. **Match to an offer only when a fit is clear.** Map their answers to your active offers ([your offers, from memory/business-context.md]) and your primary revenue path ([your primary revenue path]). If nothing fits, say so honestly.
6. **Close at the right energy.** Soft close for explorers, direct close for ready buyers. Anchor to [your primary revenue path].
7. **Handle hesitation once.** Validate, reframe, leave the door open. Do not re-pitch in the same breath.
8. **Follow up with value, capped at three touches.** Each touch must add something, not just "checking in".
9. **Respect the no.** When it comes, thank them and stop.
10. **Log the outcome** so the next conversation has context (see section 9).

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- **Always safe (act, then log):** delivering a requested lead magnet, answering a factual question about an offer, sending a warm acknowledgement, liking or replying to engage before outreach, capping and respecting a no.
- **Draft and wait for approval:** any close or pitch, any objection response that names a price, any cold DM to a named person, any message to someone who has not yet given permission, anything sent on the founder's behalf where the wording carries her reputation. Default for a team member or VA: draft, then get founder approval before sending a pitch.
- **Never, no matter the tier:** promise a result, discount, or refund without human sign-off; commit to a call time, contract, or payment; fabricate a testimonial, statistic, or case study; copy-paste a script unpersonalised; send a fourth follow-up after silence; pitch a minor or anyone whose age or capacity to consent is unclear; re-enter a sequence after a clear no.

## 7. Escalation

Escalate to the member (or, if a team member is running the inbox, to the founder) when any of these appear: a money matter (refund, dispute, payment plan), a complaint or reputational risk, anything legal or compliance-related, signs of distress or a safeguarding concern, a request that sits outside your active offers, or a buying signal from a high-value or sensitive contact you are unsure how to handle. Time-sensitive items (an angry message, a distressed person, a hot lead about to go cold) go to the founder's fastest channel now. Everything else goes in the end-of-day digest with the thread linked. Log the judgement call in `logs/decision-log.md` so the pattern can be learned.

## 8. Responsible use

This skill talks to real people in a private channel, so the failure modes are specific. Never fabricate a testimonial, result, statistic, or scarcity claim ("only 2 spots left") that is not true. Never pressure: one reframe on an objection, then the door stays open. Never pitch a minor or anyone whose ability to consent is unclear. Never treat a follower's handle as consent to sell; the codeword, download, or question is the consent. Never send a fourth follow-up into silence. When a person is in distress, drop the script and escalate to a human. If a team member or assistant is sending on the founder's behalf and the person asks, be honest that the inbox is managed by the team, do not pretend to be the founder typing live. Transparency keeps the trust that makes the next message welcome.

### Qualification Questions

Ask these before pitching, spaced naturally through the conversation. Do not fire them all at once.

1. Identify the problem: `What's the biggest thing you're struggling with when it comes to [topic area]?`
2. Understand the goal: `If you could wave a magic wand and change one thing about [their situation], what would it be?`
3. Gauge readiness: `Have you tried anything to solve this before? What worked and what didn't?`
4. Check fit: `Are you at the point where you're ready to invest in solving this properly, or still exploring?`

Map their answers to your active offers ([your offers, from memory/business-context.md]) and point toward your primary revenue path: [your primary revenue path].

```

**Direct close (for ready buyers):**

```

### Objection Responses

Validate first, reframe once, leave the door open. These cover common objections: [the objections you commonly hear].

**"I don't have time right now":**
```
I totally understand, time is the thing we never have enough of.

Can I ask, how much time are you currently spending on [the problem]? Because what I've found is this actually saves time in the long run by [specific mechanism].

No pressure at all, I'm here whenever you're ready. 💛
```

**"It's too expensive":**
```
I totally understand, it's a real investment and I want you to feel confident about it.

What I'd love you to consider is: what's the cost of staying where you are for another 3 to 6 months? My [clients/students] find the investment pays for itself through [specific outcome].

No pressure at all, I'm here whenever you're ready. 💛
```

**"I need to think about it":**
```
I totally understand, it's a big decision and I want you to feel right about it.

Of course, take the time you need. Would it help if I sent you [a case study / more details / a quick video] so you can make an informed decision?

No pressure at all, I'm here whenever you're ready. 💛
```

### Follow-Up Sequence

For people who showed interest but didn't convert. Value-led, never pushy, capped at three touches.

**Day 1, the check-in:**
```
Hey [name]! Just wanted to check in after our chat yesterday.

Did you get a chance to look at [the resource/details] I sent?

No rush at all, just here if you have any questions. 😊
```

**Day 3, the value add:**
```
Hey [name]! Thought of you when I [saw this article / created this post / recorded this tip] about [topic they mentioned].

[Share the resource or insight]

Hope it's useful! Let me know if you want to chat more about [their specific challenge].
```

**Day 7, the soft close:**
```
Hi [name]! One last follow-up from me.

I know things get busy, so I just wanted to say: if [your offer] is still on your radar, I'm here to answer any questions.

And if it's not the right time, no worries at all, I'll keep sharing on [your Instagram handle] and you're always welcome to reach out when you're ready.

Wishing you all the best! 💛
```

### Cold DM Strategy

For collaborators, podcast hosts, or ideal clients who have not engaged yet. Warm up first.

1. Engage first: like and comment genuinely on 3 to 5 of their posts over 1 to 2 weeks.
2. Find common ground: shared audience, mutual connection, similar values.
3. Open with value: lead with what you can offer, not what you want.

**Cold DM template:**
```
Hey [name]! I've been loving your content about [specific topic they post about].

[Specific compliment about a recent post or piece of work]

I'm the member, I help your ideal customer. the transformation you deliver

[Specific reason for reaching out: collaboration idea, resource to share, genuine question]

Would love to connect if you're open to it!
```

### Voice Guidelines

Do:
- Sound like the member in a one-on-one conversation.
- Use your preferred variant of English spelling.
- Match the emoji guideline: your emoji preference.
- Keep messages concise, 2 to 4 sentences in most cases.
- Use voice notes where appropriate, they add warmth.
- Sign off naturally, no formal sign-offs in DMs.
- Use the member's signature phrases where they fit naturally: [your hallmark phrases].

Don't:
- Send walls of text, break long messages into 2 or 3 shorter ones.
- Use copy-paste scripts without personalising.
- Push for a sale before understanding their situation.
- Be overly formal or stiff, DMs are casual by nature.
- Send follow-ups too frequently, respect their time and inbox.
- Use banned words: game changer, deep dive, synergy, hustle, grind.

### Banned Words & Phrases

Never use these in any content:

- game changer
- deep dive
- synergy
- leverage (as verb)
- hustle / grind
- low-hanging fruit
- unlock / skyrocket / supercharge

Add your own banned words in `memory/business-context.md`; they override this default list.

### Voice Non-Negotiables

1. Always sound like the member wrote it: conversational, not corporate
2. Use first person naturally
3. Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones
4. Include personal context or story where relevant
5. Reference expertise naturally without bragging
6. Never start with "I'm excited to announce" or "I'm thrilled to share"
7. Never sound like a press release or marketing copy
8. Never be condescending or preachy
9. The member's preferred English variant spelling throughout: no exceptions
10. When uncertain about tone, err on the side of being more human, not more polished

## 11. What good looks like

### Good example (annotated)

A follower comments the codeword, downloads the guide, then says: "Honestly I just feel like I'm drowning in admin and never get to the actual work."

Reply sent:
```
Oh I hear you, admin has a way of eating the whole day. 💛 [1]

Out of everything on your plate, what's the one task you'd hand off tomorrow if you could? [2]
```
Why it is good: [1] validates the real feeling first and matches a warm, human voice instead of pivoting straight to a pitch. [2] asks one open qualification question (the rubric's "shared a problem but not a goal" move) rather than four at once. It honours the governing principle: the next message is earned, not assumed. No offer yet, because no fit is clear yet.

Three audiences, same shape: a **founder** turns this into a [your primary revenue path] conversation; a **professional** uses the identical pattern to qualify a LinkedIn enquiry before booking a call; in **real life** the same "validate, then ask one question" move handles a community or family message without it feeling transactional.

### Bad example (named failure mode)

```
Hi! Saw you downloaded my guide. My program is $2,000 and spots are almost gone, only 2 left! Want the link? It'll change your life.
```
Named failure mode: **interruption marketing pretending to be a conversation** (the exact thing Godin's permission framework warns against). It pitches before earning the next message, fabricates scarcity ("only 2 left"), promises a result ("change your life"), and asks nothing about the person. It treats a download as consent to sell. This is the highest-risk pattern in the inbox: it burns trust, gets muted, and breaches the never-do list in section 6.
