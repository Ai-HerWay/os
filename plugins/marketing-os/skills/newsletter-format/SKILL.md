# Skill: Newsletter Format and Structure

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill owns the shape of the member's newsletter, your newsletter, end to end: the structure of each send, the voice it is written in, the design it ships in, and the rule about what earns a place and what does not. It takes raw ideas, drafts, and content pillars and turns them into a finished, scannable, single-minded email that sounds like its author and moves one priority forward. It works the same for a founder building an audience, a professional running an internal or industry update, and a person sharing a personal or community letter. It does not invent results, send on anyone's behalf, or change the brand without approval.

## 2. Governing principle

One newsletter, one main idea: every send earns its place by making one point land clearly, and anything that competes with that point gets cut or saved for next time.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

People do not read newsletters, they scan them. Nielsen Norman Group's eye-tracking research (Pernice, 2017, building on the original 2006 study of 232 users) found readers move through screen content in an F-shaped pattern: two horizontal sweeps near the top, then a vertical scan down the left edge. Whatever sits below or to the right of that path is often missed. This is why the structure below front-loads the point, uses descriptive H2 and H3 headers, and keeps the first words of each section meaningful: it puts the value where the eye actually travels.

Nielsen Norman Group's dedicated report, Marketing Email and Newsletter Usability (NN/g, multiple editions), distils 199 evidence-based guidelines from usability testing and lands on a consistent message: clarity, scannability, and a single obvious purpose beat density and decoration. Cluttered, multi-message emails underperform because they ask the reader to choose what matters, and readers under time pressure often choose nothing.

The single-idea rule rests on choice overload. Iyengar and Lepper's well-known 2000 study, "When Choice is Demotivating", found shoppers offered 24 jam varieties were far less likely to buy (3%) than those offered six (about 40%), even though the larger display drew more initial attention. More options drew the eye but stalled the decision. A newsletter crammed with five competing calls to action recreates the 24-jam table: high attention, low action. One clear ask, supported by one main idea, is the six-jam table.

Plainly stated: the F-pattern and NN/g usability work are established, named research; the single-idea principle is supported by the Iyengar and Lepper choice-overload framework rather than by a study run specifically on newsletters, and it is applied here by analogy. We name that openly rather than overclaim.

## 4. The decision rubric

This is the psychology layer: how the skill decides what goes in, what gets cut, and how a send is shaped. Defaults can be overridden in `memory/business-context.md`.

| Condition the skill sees | Default decision |
|---|---|
| Draft has more than one main idea competing for the headline | Pick the strongest single idea, cut or defer the rest, note the deferred idea for next issue |
| Two or more calls to action of similar weight | Keep one primary CTA aligned to the current business priority, demote the rest to a single inline mention or remove |
| Section does not serve this issue's one idea | Cut it, even if it is well written (save to working memory for a future send) |
| Subject line over 60 characters or vague | Rewrite to 40 to 60 characters with curiosity or a specific promise, avoid spam triggers (free, urgent, limited time) |
| Opening leads with the offer or the author, not the reader | Rewrite to open with the reader's problem or a relatable moment, then bridge to the idea |
| Body is dense paragraphs with no headers | Add descriptive H2/H3 headers and front-load the first sentence of each section (F-pattern) |
| Idea is genuinely big and complex | Split across two issues rather than overloading one, keep each send single-minded |
| Resource, community, or lead-magnet section is empty this week | Omit the section cleanly rather than padding it |
| Tone drifts from the member's brand voice | Pull back to as defined in your voice rules before structure work, voice first |
| Member has set a custom rule in business-context.md | The member's rule overrides this default |

Edge case: if the member explicitly wants a digest or round-up format (many short items, no single idea), honour it, but still enforce one primary CTA and clear scannable headers.

## 5. Workflow

1. Read the inputs (Section 9): business-context.md, the member's voice and pillars, any working-memory drafts or idea list for this issue.
2. Identify the one main idea for this send. If several compete, apply the rubric and defer the rest.
3. Choose the primary CTA, the single most important action aligned to the current business priority.
4. Draft the subject line (40 to 60 characters) and a preview-friendly first line.
5. Write the personal opening: open with the reader's moment or problem, bridge to the idea, in the member's voice.
6. Build the main content around the one idea, using descriptive headers so the first words carry meaning (F-pattern check).
7. Add only the supporting sections that serve this issue (resource, community, footer). Omit empty ones cleanly.
8. Place the primary CTA where the scan path lands, and remove competing asks.
9. Run the scannability pass: read only the headers and first sentences, does the point still land?
10. Apply design guidelines and the platform checklist (mobile preview, send time, deliverability).
11. Prepare the repurposing plan (Section 10) so the send compounds into social content.
12. Log the draft and any structural decisions to the activity and decision logs.

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- Always safe (act, then log): drafting the newsletter, restructuring for scannability, tightening to one idea, rewriting subject lines, proposing the repurposing plan, checking against design guidelines.
- Draft and wait for approval: changing the established newsletter structure or template, changing the brand voice or design tokens, adding or swapping a primary CTA tied to a business priority, anything that references results or testimonials.
- Never (no matter the tier): send the newsletter without explicit approval, invent subscriber numbers, testimonials, reader wins, or results, commit to an offer or price, delete the member's drafts or list data, act outside the newsletter scope.

## 7. Escalation

When unsure, the skill asks before acting and routes by urgency. Send-blocking questions (a factual claim that cannot be verified, a missing CTA decision, a draft that contradicts brand voice) go to the member in their time-sensitive channel before the send. Non-blocking observations (a recurring structural weakness, a pillar that keeps underperforming) go to the end-of-day digest. Any decision to change structure, voice, or design is written to the decision log and flagged for the member to review.

## 8. Responsible use

Tied to this skill's real failure modes: never fabricate subscriber counts, open or click rates, reader testimonials, or "wins" for the community section. If a number or quote is not supplied or verifiable, leave a clearly marked gap for the member to fill, do not invent one. Never add affiliate links the member has not approved. Never send on the member's behalf without explicit sign-off. When this skill drafts content that will go to real subscribers, the output is a draft for human review, and the member remains the author and sender of record.

## 11. What good looks like

### Good example (annotated)

> Subject: The email I almost did not send (47 chars)  [1]
>
> Last Tuesday I deleted a whole newsletter draft. Three ideas, two offers, a tool review, and a reader story all fighting for the same 600 words. I sat back and realised: if I could not say what this email was about in one line, neither could my reader.  [2]
>
> ## So here is the one thing this week
> Pick the single idea. Cut the rest. I now write the subject line first and refuse to start drafting until that line says one clear thing.  [3]
>
> ## How I do it in three steps
> 1. Write the one-line promise. 2. Build only the sections that serve it. 3. Read just the headers, if the point survives, send it.
>
> ## Try this
> Open your last draft and count the calls to action. If there is more than one, you have your homework.  [4]

[1] Subject is under 60 characters, curiosity plus a specific promise, no spam triggers.
[2] Opening starts with the reader-relatable problem (a cluttered draft), not the author or an offer, then bridges to the idea.
[3] One main idea, stated plainly, reinforced by a descriptive H2 so it lands on the F-pattern scan.
[4] A single primary CTA, low friction, aligned to the idea, no competing asks.

Three audiences, same structure: a founder runs this as a growth newsletter with a Hub CTA, a professional runs it as a fortnightly team or industry update with one decision to action, and in real life it is a community or family letter with one story and one invitation. The skill does not fork, only the one idea and the CTA change.

### Bad example (named failure mode: choice overload)

> Subject: June update, free guide, new podcast, sale ends Friday, plus reader Q&A and my favourite tools!!!
>
> Hey everyone, lots to get through today so let us get into it...
> [then five sections, four different links, three CTAs of equal weight, and no single point]

Failure mode: choice overload (the 24-jam table). The subject line is a list, every section competes, and the reader is asked to choose what matters across multiple equal asks. Per Iyengar and Lepper, more options draw attention but stall action, so this send gets opened and then abandoned. The fix is the governing principle: one idea, one primary CTA, cut the rest.
