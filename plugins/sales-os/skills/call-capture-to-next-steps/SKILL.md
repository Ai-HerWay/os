# Skill: Call Capture to Next Steps

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill owns the moment after a sales conversation ends for your business: it turns raw notes or a transcript into a clean, factual record, the key things learned, a staged pipeline or CRM update, and one clear, dated next step. It works for the person who had the conversation (a founder writing up a discovery call, a professional producing forecast-grade CRM notes a manager will rely on, or someone in real life capturing the outcome of a chat with a potential collaborator). It owns the capture and the staged update; it does not commit anything to the CRM-of-record and it does not send anything to the buyer. Its job is to make sure that what happened is recorded accurately and the deal has a real next step, so nothing drifts because nobody wrote it down.

## 2. Governing principle

Never record a detail the buyer did not say. Everything in the capture is marked stated-by-buyer or inferred, and an inferred line is never promoted to a fact. An auto-logged summary that is subtly wrong becomes a permanent record that pollutes the forecast, so accuracy beats completeness every time.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

Three named bodies of work underpin this skill.

**A clear, recommended next step after a call is what makes deals progress.** Gong Labs analysis of sales calls (2024 to 2025) found that reps who complete clear, specific next steps after a call progress and win at materially higher rates than those who leave next steps vague or absent. Note the selection bias: this is data from Gong's own user base, so read it directionally rather than as a universal law. The directional lesson is robust and it is why this skill always ends in one dated next step, never a vague "follow up sometime". Source: Gong Labs analysis.

**Most CRM data is not trusted, and reps barely sell.** Salesforce State of Sales reports that only about 35 per cent of sales professionals trust their CRM data, and that reps lose a large share of the week to admin and data entry rather than selling (the widely-cited figure of around 28 per cent of time actually spent selling comes from Salesforce State of Sales, 2022). The lesson: untrusted, half-entered notes are worse than useless, and the fix is auto-capturing the conversation into clean, consistent fields so the record is both fast to produce and worth trusting. This is the case for the staged update format in Section 10. Source: Salesforce State of Sales (2022 figure cited as such).

**Post-call summaries are among the clearest wins from generative AI in sales.** McKinsey's "An unconstrained future: How generative AI could reshape B2B sales" (September 2024) identifies post-interaction summaries and CRM updates as among the clearest, best-evidenced gains from generative AI in sales, precisely because they are high-frequency, time-consuming, and well suited to drafting that a human then approves. This is the named basis for the draft-and-approve autonomy in Section 6. Source: McKinsey, "An unconstrained future: How generative AI could reshape B2B sales", September 2024.

Three audiences, same evidence: a **founder** writes up a discovery call into an honest record and a real next step, a **professional** produces forecast-grade CRM notes the manager expects, and in **real life** the same shape captures the outcome of a conversation with a potential collaborator so the agreement does not evaporate.

## 4. The decision rubric

This is the psychology layer. Run each condition against the default before producing the capture. The override column wins when it applies.

| Condition the skill looks for | Default decision | Edge case that overrides |
|---|---|---|
| A detail is in your head but not in the notes or transcript | Mark it inferred, never stated. Do not write it as a buyer fact | The buyer said it elsewhere in the same source and you can point to where |
| A budget, timeline, or authority figure is implied but not spoken | Record as inferred with the basis ("inferred from: they mentioned a board sign-off") | The buyer stated it plainly; then record as stated |
| Notes are thin, partial, or contradictory | Capture what is there, flag the gaps, do not fill them with plausible guesses | None. A confidently wrong record is worse than an honest gap |
| No clear next step emerged from the call | Stage a next step and a date, flagged as proposed for the human to confirm | The call ended with the buyer declining; then the next step is to honour that |
| A pipeline stage change is implied | Stage the new stage but do not commit it; the human moves the deal | None. Stage movement is forecast-grade and stays a human commit |
| The buyer signalled a real deadline or limit | Record it exactly as stated, with the source | None. Never round up or sharpen a deadline the buyer gave softly |
| The buyer seemed hesitant, confused, or a poor fit | Flag it as a risk in the capture, do not bury it to flatter the deal | None. The risk line exists precisely to keep the forecast honest |
| Sensitive personal detail came up (health, finances, personal life) | Capture only what is relevant to the deal; leave the rest out | None. Least personal data needed for the task |
| The transcript attributes a quote you are not sure of | Attribute cautiously or leave unattributed; never invent who said what | The source clearly labels the speaker |

## 5. Workflow

1. Read inputs (Section 9) before writing anything: the member's business or job context, the prospect insights, the existing pipeline or connected CRM, and the raw notes or transcript for this call. Know the current deal stage before you propose moving it.
2. Build the factual summary from the source only. The implicit move: separate what was said from what you are reading into it, line by line, before you write a word of the capture.
3. Extract what we learned: the real problem, decision criteria, who is involved, timeline, and any budget signal. Tag each one stated-by-buyer or inferred, with the basis for every inferred line.
4. Decide the next step: one specific action with a real date. If the call gave you no next step, propose one and flag it for the human to confirm.
5. Stage the pipeline or CRM update: the stage (unchanged or proposed new stage), the next step, and the next-step date, formatted for the human to commit. Do not commit it yourself.
6. Flag any risk: hesitation, a missing stakeholder, a soft timeline, a fit concern. Surface it plainly so the forecast stays honest.
7. Pressure-test the whole capture against the rubric, with special attention to every line being correctly marked stated or inferred. Then log the capture and the staged update.

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- **Always safe (act, then log):** draft the factual summary, extract and tag what we learned, draft the staged pipeline or CRM update, propose a dated next step, flag risks, and write the capture and staged update to working files.
- **Draft and wait for approval:** committing the update to the CRM-of-record, moving the deal stage in the system, and anything that goes to the buyer (a recap email, a calendar invite, a proposal). These are draft-and-approve; the human commits and the human sends.
- **Never (no matter the tier):** fabricate a buyer detail, quote, budget, or commitment the source does not contain; manufacture urgency or sharpen a soft deadline; pressure the buyer or write a pushy next step; move money or commit a price, discount, or contract; auto-commit to the CRM-of-record or send anything to the buyer; act below the approval tier.

## 7. Escalation

When unsure, route by stakes. Anything that changes the forecast (a stage move, a budget figure, a close date) is staged but flagged to the member before it is committed, because these are the honesty-critical numbers. If the notes and the existing record conflict (the buyer said something that contradicts what is in the CRM), stop and surface both to the member rather than overwriting silently. If the buyer seemed vulnerable, confused, or a poor fit, raise it in the capture's risk line and in the decision log. Routine, clean captures go in the activity log and the end-of-day digest. If a critical detail (budget, authority, timeline) cannot be confirmed from the source, leave it blank and say so; never fill the gap to make the record look complete.

## 8. Responsible use

Specific to this skill's failure modes: never invent a detail the buyer did not say, never promote an inferred line to a stated fact, never attribute a quote you are unsure of, never sharpen a soft timeline or budget signal into a hard commitment, and never bury a risk to make a deal look healthier than it is. The core danger here is quiet and compounding: an auto-logged summary that is subtly wrong becomes a permanent record, and a wrong record pollutes the forecast and the next conversation. Honest gaps are always better than confident guesses. The human commits the CRM update and the human sends anything to the buyer. When a recap or note is produced with AI assistance and goes out, keep that transparent in line with the member's disclosure preference.

## 9. Inputs and memory

- **Reads:** `memory/business-context.md` (or `memory/job-context.md`) for the member's business, offer, voice, and overridable thresholds; `memory/prospect-insights.md` for what is already known about this buyer and account; `memory/sales-pipeline.md` or the connected CRM for the current deal, stage, and history; and the raw notes or transcript for this call.
- **Writes:** `logs/activity-log.md` (what was captured and staged), `logs/decision-log.md` (any flagged stage move, budget figure, close date, or risk for review), plus the call capture and the staged CRM update. The human commits the staged update to the CRM-of-record.

Never read "any relevant context". Read the named files above.

## 10. Output format

The deliverable is the Call Capture block below. Keep this structure, the section order, and every placeholder. Every line in "What we learned" is marked (stated) or (inferred).

---

# Call Capture: {{prospectName}} ({{accountName}})

> Captured for the member on {{callDate}}. A factual record of the conversation. Inferred lines are marked and never treated as fact. The staged update is for you to commit.

---

## Factual summary

{{factualSummary}} (what happened, drawn only from the notes or transcript)

---

## What we learned

- **Real problem:** {{realProblem}} ({{problemSource}})
- **Decision criteria:** {{decisionCriteria}} ({{criteriaSource}})
- **Who is involved:** {{stakeholders}} ({{stakeholderSource}})
- **Timeline:** {{timeline}} ({{timelineSource}})
{{#if budgetSignal}}- **Budget signal:** {{budgetSignal}} ({{budgetSource}}){{/if}}

Each source tag reads either (stated) or (inferred: basis). No line is promoted from inferred to stated.

---

## Staged pipeline / CRM update (for you to commit)

- **Current stage:** {{currentStage}}
- **Proposed stage:** {{proposedStage}} (unchanged unless the call genuinely moved the deal)
- **Next step:** {{nextStep}}
- **Next-step date:** {{nextStepDate}}

This is staged, not committed. You move the deal and commit the fields.

---

## Risk to flag

{{riskFlag}} (hesitation, a missing decision-maker, a soft timeline, a fit concern, or "none observed")

## 11. What good looks like

**Good example (annotated).**

> Factual summary: 30-minute discovery call. The buyer described losing two days a week to manual reporting and said the current tool "just does not talk to anything else". [1] What we learned: Real problem: reporting is manual and disconnected (stated). Decision criteria: integration with their existing stack came up twice (stated); ease of onboarding (inferred: they asked how long setup takes). [2] Who is involved: the buyer plus "my ops lead and probably finance" (stated). Timeline: "before the new financial year" (stated). Budget signal: none stated. [3] Staged update: stage stays Discovery, proposed next step: send a short integration overview and book a follow-up; next-step date: {{nextStepDate}}. Risk to flag: finance is named but absent, so budget authority is unconfirmed. [4]

1. The summary is built only from what was said; nothing is added for colour.
2. The criteria are split cleanly: integration is marked stated, ease of onboarding is marked inferred with the basis, per the governing principle.
3. Budget is left as "none stated" rather than guessed, which keeps the forecast honest.
4. The next step is specific and dated (per Gong Labs analysis), and a real risk is surfaced rather than buried.

Across the three audiences this holds: a **founder** turns a discovery call into an honest record and a real next step, a **professional** produces forecast-grade CRM notes with every field marked stated or inferred, and in **real life** the same capture records what a potential collaborator actually agreed to and when the next conversation happens.

**Bad example (named failure mode: inventing a detail the buyer did not say).**

> What we learned: Budget: around $50k, ready to sign this quarter. Decision: the buyer can approve it herself. Next step: follow up soon.

Failure mode: fabricated buyer detail polluting the forecast. The buyer never stated a $50k budget or that she alone could approve it; both were inferred and then written as facts, and the next step has no date. This is exactly the subtly-wrong record the governing principle forbids: once committed it inflates the forecast, misleads the next conversation, and is almost impossible to unwind. The skill must refuse this and route to the honest pattern above: mark every line stated or inferred, leave unknowns blank, and end with one dated next step.
