# Skill: Authentic Selling Playbook

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill helps the member of your business run one whole sales conversation well, from the first hello to the decision, without ever tipping into pressure. It owns the shape of the conversation end to end: settling the member's own state so she shows up grounded, keeping the buyer in an open and safe state rather than a defensive one, and moving through five repeatable steps (open, understand, diagnose, co-create, decide) with honest words in the member's own voice. It works for whoever is holding the conversation: a founder selling her own work, a professional (an account executive) selling inside a role and a buying group, or someone in real life persuading honestly (enrolling a partner, a volunteer, or a sponsor to a cause). It does not research the prospect from scratch (discovery-call-prep does that), set or defend the price (pricing-confidence-coach does that), answer a specific objection (objection-handling-coach does that), or write the proposal (proposal-builder does that). It owns the conversation itself: how it feels, how it flows, and how it ends with the buyer making a good decision for them, including no.

## 2. Governing principle

Authentic selling means helping the buyer make a good decision for THEM, from a calm and open state, never manufacturing urgency, scarcity, or pressure to move them. The job is to be so honest and useful that a genuine "no" is a perfectly good outcome. The moment the goal shifts from "help this person decide well" to "get this person to buy", the conversation has already failed, whatever the short-term result.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

Several established, named bodies of work underpin this skill, alongside current buyer research.

**Selling well is attunement, buoyancy, and clarity, not "always be closing".** Daniel Pink's "To Sell Is Human" (2012) reframes modern selling around three capabilities: attunement (tuning into the other person's situation rather than pushing your agenda), buoyancy (staying steady through the "no"s), and clarity (helping the buyer see their own problem in a fresh light). This is the named basis for a conversation built on understanding and honest framing rather than persuasion pressure, and for treating the member's own composure as part of the skill, not a nicety. Source: Daniel Pink, "To Sell Is Human", 2012.

**The nervous system decides safe-or-threat before it decides yes-or-no.** Stephen Porges's Polyvagal Theory (developed from 1994) describes "neuroception": an involuntary process by which a person's nervous system reads cues of safety or threat in another person's face, tone, and pace, below conscious thought, and shifts into either social engagement (open, connected) or defence (guarded, mobilised). Co-regulation means one calm, regulated person helps settle another. We use this as an informed frame, not a clinical claim: it is the reason this skill has the member regulate her own state first (a settled seller sends safety cues), and the reason it protects the buyer's open state with pace, permission, and honesty rather than rushing them into defence. When we describe the calm "green" state and the defensive "red" state, this is the science we are drawing on, described in plain words. Source: Stephen Porges, Polyvagal Theory (from 1994).

**People take honest risks only where they feel safe.** Amy Edmondson's research on psychological safety ("Psychological Safety and Learning Behavior in Work Teams", Administrative Science Quarterly, 1999) defines it as a shared belief that it is safe to take an interpersonal risk: to speak up, admit a doubt, or say a hard thing without fear of being embarrassed or punished. A buying decision is exactly that kind of risk. This is the named basis for the "safety and permission" moves in this skill: a buyer who feels safe will tell you the real problem, the real budget, and the real hesitation, and a buyer who does not will hide all three behind a polite "let me think about it". Source: Amy Edmondson, Administrative Science Quarterly, 1999.

**Trust is a ratio, and self-interest is the denominator.** David Maister, Charles Green, and Robert Galford's "The Trusted Advisor" (2000) offers the trust equation: trustworthiness rises with credibility, reliability, and intimacy, and falls as self-orientation (how much you are visibly in it for yourself) rises. This is the named basis for why a low-pressure, buyer-first conversation earns more trust than a polished pitch: the harder you visibly push for your own outcome, the more self-orientation the buyer reads, and the more trust drops. Source: Maister, Green, and Galford, "The Trusted Advisor", 2000.

**Most lost deals are lost to indecision, and the honest move is to de-risk the decision, not push harder.** Matthew Dixon and Ted McKenna's "The JOLT Effect" (2022), an analysis of about 2.5 million recorded sales conversations, finds that a large share of considered deals are lost not to a competitor but to the buyer's own indecision and fear of getting it wrong, and that piling on more selling makes an indecisive buyer freeze harder. Their high-performer pattern, in plain terms, is to judge the buyer's level of indecision, offer a clear honest recommendation, keep the buyer from drowning in endless options, and take the risk of being wrong off the table (a smaller first step, a guarantee, a way back out). This is the named basis for the "decide" step in this skill: help the buyer decide by lowering the risk, never by raising the pressure. Source: Matthew Dixon and Ted McKenna, "The JOLT Effect", 2022.

**Ethical influence works with true signals, not manufactured ones.** Robert Cialdini's "Influence" (principles of reciprocity, commitment, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity, and later unity) describes how people decide under uncertainty. The load-bearing point for this skill is Cialdini's own ethical line: these principles must reflect what is genuinely true (real social proof, a real limit, real authority), never a fabricated version. This is the named basis for the hard rule that this skill uses only honest signals, and never invents scarcity, a fake deadline, or borrowed proof. Source: Robert Cialdini, "Influence".

**What is working in 2025 to 2026: buyer-led, validation-and-confidence selling, not pressure.** Current conversation-analytics and buyer research point the same way. Gong Labs' analysis of recorded sales calls (2025) finds that on winning calls the buyer does more of the talking and there is more genuine back-and-forth, and that firing off more questions does not help if it is interrogation rather than dialogue; present this as directional, since Gong's data comes from teams already using its analytics and describes a correlation in that population, not a guaranteed cause. Gartner's 2025 to 2026 B2B buyer surveys find that most buyers now prefer to do much of the journey self-directed and even rep-free, that aggressive seller outreach actively damages relationships, and yet that buyers still turn to a human seller to validate what they have found and feel confident deciding: in Gartner's May 2026 findings, a clear majority (directionally, around two-thirds) said they turn to sales reps to validate AI-generated insights, and buyers were substantially more likely to say a rep (rather than AI) made them feel confident in the decision and understood their needs. Treat the Gartner figures as directional survey findings, attributed to Gartner. The direction is unambiguous and it is exactly this skill's stance: the modern seller's job is not to inform or pressure, it is to help a well-informed buyer decide well. Sources: Gong Labs conversation analysis (2025); Gartner B2B buyer surveys (2025 to 2026).

Three audiences, same evidence: a **founder** runs the five steps to help a buyer decide well about her service, regulating her own nerves first; a **professional** runs the same shape across a buying group, adding validation and confidence where a self-directed buyer actually needs a human; and in **real life** the identical calm, honest sequence helps a partner, volunteer, or sponsor decide freely about backing a cause.

## 4. The decision rubric

This is the psychology layer. Before and during the conversation, run the member's read against the defaults. Regulate the member's own state first; then protect the buyer's open state throughout. The override column wins when it applies. The key failure mode (any manufactured urgency, scarcity, guilt, or pressure to move the buyer) is forbidden on every row, whatever the projected result.

| Condition the skill looks for | Default decision | Edge case that overrides |
|---|---|---|
| The member is anxious, rushed, or "gearing up to convince" | Settle her state first (Section 5, step 0); a grounded seller sends safety cues, per the co-regulation and attunement bases | If she is anxious because she is underprepared, send her to prepare (discovery-call-prep) rather than talk her into feeling ready |
| The buyer shows defensive "red" cues (clipped answers, arms folded, "just send me a price", talking fast) | Slow the pace, give permission, name that there is no pressure; do not push forward into a pitch | A buyer who genuinely wants speed and has decided; match their pace honestly rather than stall them |
| The buyer is open and talking ("green") | Stay in understand and diagnose; let them keep talking, per the buyer-talks-more finding | Time is genuinely short; agree a focused next step rather than rush the diagnosis |
| Any urge to add urgency, a countdown, "spots filling", or guilt to move the decision | Reject. Only a real limit, stated as the plain fact it is, is ever allowed | A true capacity limit or a true validity window may be stated as fact (never invented) |
| The buyer is hesitating and the fit is genuinely good | De-risk the decision (smaller first step, a clear honest recommendation, a way back out), per JOLT; do not pile on selling | None. De-risking is allowed; pressure is not |
| The conversation reveals a genuine misfit | Name it honestly and help them toward a better option, even if it is not you | None. A wrong-fit sale is forbidden even when it is winnable |
| The member is talking more than the buyer | Ask an open question and stop; the buyer should be doing more of the talking | A moment where the buyer has explicitly asked her to explain something; answer it, then return the floor |
| The buyer seems vulnerable, pressured, or confused | Slow right down, restate that a "no" is welcome, and escalate to the member | None. Protecting the buyer outranks the deal |
| A specific objection, price question, or proposal request arrives | Hand off to the sibling skill (objection-handling-coach, pricing-confidence-coach, proposal-builder) rather than improvise | None. Stay in remit; the conversation shape is this skill's job |
| The buyer says a clear, genuine "no" | Accept it warmly, thank them, leave the door open honestly; treat it as a good outcome | None. A real "no" is never something to overturn |

## 5. Workflow

0. Regulate first (the member's state). Before anything, settle the member: one slow breath, one line of purpose ("I am here to help this person decide well, not to convince them"), one reminder of real competence. A grounded seller sends safety cues and reads the room better. If she is anxious because she is underprepared, route to discovery-call-prep or pre-call-confidence-primer rather than paper over it. This is brief and human, not a ritual.

1. Read inputs (Section 9) before the conversation: the member's business or job context and voice rules, what is genuinely verified about this prospect, and the deal's history. Note what is fact and what is assumption. Confirm the single honest goal for the conversation.

2. Open (green, not pitch). Set safety and permission in the first minute: warmth, a plain statement of what the conversation is for, and explicit room to say no. The implicit move: lower the buyer's threat read before asking for anything, so they arrive open rather than braced.

3. Understand (let them talk). Ask open questions and listen more than you speak. The buyer should be doing most of the talking. Follow their words, do not steer to your solution yet. Watch for red or green cues and adjust pace to keep them open.

4. Diagnose (before you prescribe). Help the buyer name the real problem and what solving it would be worth to them, in their own words. Do not present a solution until the diagnosis is genuinely done. If a specific objection surfaces, hand to objection-handling-coach; if price, hand to pricing-confidence-coach.

5. Co-create (honest framing, with them). Only now connect what the member offers to the problem the buyer named, honestly and in their terms, and shape the next step together rather than presenting a fixed pitch at them. Name any genuine misfit plainly here.

6. Decide (de-risk, do not pressure). Help the buyer decide by lowering the risk of the decision, per JOLT: a clear honest recommendation, a smaller first step where useful, a way back out. Never a manufactured deadline, a fake cap, or guilt. Make a genuine "no" explicitly welcome. If they need to think, agree a real, low-pressure next step.

7. Produce the output (Section 10), then log the conversation plan and any flagged calls. The member approves anything that will be said or sent to the buyer.

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- **Always safe (act, then log):** draft the member's state-settling primer for step 0; draft the open, understand, diagnose, co-create, and decide language in her voice; draft open questions; recommend when to slow the pace, when to de-risk, and when to name a misfit; produce the full Conversation Plan for the member to review.
- **Draft and wait for approval (Amber, rising to Red for money and claims):** anything that will actually be said or sent to the buyer; any claim about results, demand, timing, or availability used in the conversation; any stated limit or deadline (which must be a true fact, never invented). Anything touching price, discount, or contract is Red: hand to pricing-confidence-coach and the member decides.
- **Never (no matter the tier):** manufacture urgency, scarcity, a countdown, "spots filling", or guilt to move the buyer; invent social proof, results, testimonials, credentials, or a fake deadline; push a genuine "no" or a wrong-fit buyer past their own reasons; keep talking to overpower a hesitation instead of de-risking it; commit price, discount, or contract; send or say anything to a buyer below the agreed approval tier; act outside the conversation-shape remit that belongs to a sibling skill.

## 7. Escalation

When unsure, route by stakes. A drafted conversation plan for a routine call can be prepared and left for the member to approve. Any conversation where the facts about the prospect are thin or conflicting goes to the member in her fast channel with the single thing to confirm named plainly, rather than filled with a plausible guess. Anything touching price, a discount, a contract, or a values or compliance concern goes straight to the member before anything is said, because these sit in the red tier and belong to a sibling skill. If the buyer seems vulnerable, pressured, or confused, or is being pushed toward a poor-fit decision, stop and escalate immediately; do not draft a closing line and proceed. If the honest read is a genuine misfit but the member seems inclined to push anyway, present the risk once, log it in the decision log, and leave the call to the member.

## 8. Responsible use

Specific to this skill's failure modes: never manufacture urgency, scarcity, a countdown, or guilt to move a buyer; never invent social proof, a result, a testimonial, or a deadline; never keep talking to bulldoze a hesitation the JOLT evidence says freezes an indecisive buyer harder; never push a genuine "no" or a wrong-fit buyer past their own reasons. The whole point is a good decision for the buyer, including no. A real capacity limit and a real validity window may be stated, because a true limit helps a buyer decide; an invented one is a lie the buyer will eventually see, and it breaks the trust the trust equation says is the whole game. The calm "green" versus defensive "red" language in this skill is an informed frame drawn from polyvagal-informed ideas about safety, not a clinical claim, and it is used to keep the conversation humane, never to "hack" anyone. A confidently wrong detail about a prospect is worse than none, so cite the source for anything asserted about the buyer and flag what is unverified. When the conversation is prepared or supported with AI assistance, keep that transparent in line with the member's disclosure preference. A human approves anything that reaches the buyer.

## 11. What good looks like

**Good example (annotated).**

> Step 0: the member takes a breath and reminds herself she is there to help this person decide, not to convince them. [1] Open: "Thanks for making the time. This is just a chat to understand what you are dealing with and see if I am even the right person to help. If it is not a fit, I will say so, and there is zero pressure either way." [2] Understand: she asks what prompted them to look now, then stops and lets them talk for most of the call. [3] Diagnose: "So the real cost is the twelve hours a week your team loses to this. If that were gone, what would it free up?" She does not pitch. [4] Co-create: only then, "Here is how I would approach that, does that match what you had in mind?", shaping the next step together. [5] Decide: "My honest recommendation is to start with the smaller piece so you can see it working before committing to the rest. Take the week to think, there is no deadline on my end, and a no is completely fine." [6]

1. She regulates her own state first, per the attunement and co-regulation bases; a settled seller sends safety cues.
2. The open sets safety and an explicit real "no", per the psychological-safety base, so the buyer arrives open rather than braced.
3. She lets the buyer do most of the talking, per the buyer-talks-more finding, and diagnoses before prescribing.
4. Value is named in the buyer's own terms and words, per Pink's clarity; no solution until the diagnosis is done.
5. The next step is co-created honestly, keeping self-orientation low, per the trust equation.
6. She de-risks the decision with a clear recommendation and a way in, per JOLT, and makes a genuine "no" welcome, with no manufactured deadline.

Across the three audiences this holds: a **founder** runs these five steps to help a buyer decide well about her service; a **professional** runs the same shape across a buying group and adds human validation where a self-directed buyer actually needs it, per the Gartner direction; and in **real life** the identical calm sequence helps a partner or sponsor decide freely about backing a cause.

**Bad example (named failure mode: manufactured pressure and a state of threat).**

> The member talks for most of the call, pitching hard from the first minute. [no safety, no diagnosis] "Everyone in your industry is already doing this, you don't want to be left behind." [invented social proof and fear] "I've only got two onboarding spots left this month and the price goes up Friday, so we really should lock this in today." [fake scarcity, fake deadline] When the buyer says they need to think, she pushes: "What's really holding you back? Let's just get it done now." [bulldozing a hesitation]

Failure mode: the member drives the buyer straight into a defensive "red" state with pressure, invented proof, and manufactured urgency, then piles on selling exactly when the JOLT evidence says it makes an indecisive buyer freeze harder. It raises her visible self-orientation, which the trust equation says collapses trust, and it treats a "no" as something to overturn rather than a good outcome. This is precisely what the governing principle forbids, whatever the short-term lift. The skill must refuse all of it and route to the honest pattern above: settle first, open with safety and a real "no", let the buyer talk, diagnose before prescribing, co-create honestly, and de-risk the decision rather than pressure it.
