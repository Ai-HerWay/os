# Skill: Internal Selling (influence without authority)

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill helps a professional win internal buy-in for an idea, a project, or a budget from people they do not manage: a manager, an executive, cross-functional peers, a deal desk, a steering group. It owns the full case for change from first framing to a ready-to-pitch plan. It frames the idea in the language of the organisation's stated priorities, maps every stakeholder and what each one cares about and fears, builds the evidence-backed business case, anticipates objections with honest responses, identifies the internal advocate worth recruiting, and proposes a low-risk pilot that shrinks the perceived risk of saying yes. It does not have the conversations for you and it does not commit the organisation to anything. It prepares the strongest honest case, then hands you a person to talk to and a plan to talk through. It works the same for a founder pitching a partner, supplier, or investor, and in real life for getting a committee or family behind a plan, but the professional selling inside a larger organisation is who it is built for first.

## 2. Governing principle

Persuade only with the truth: frame the idea to what the organisation and each person genuinely cares about, never misrepresent the facts, your evidence, or anyone else's position, and never go around a person dishonestly. Influence is earned through credibility and a real case, not through politicking, pressure, or manipulation. If winning would require any of those, do not win.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

Three named bodies of work underpin this skill.

**Influence without authority rests on credibility, framing, evidence, advocates, and reducing risk.** Jay Conger's "Exerting Influence Without Authority" (Harvard Business Review, February 2008), together with Harvard Business School Online guidance on influencing without authority, sets out that when you cannot order an outcome you must earn it: build credibility and personal capital first, frame your idea in terms of the priorities of the people you are persuading rather than your own, support the case with evidence, recruit advocates who carry weight with the group, and reduce the perceived risk of saying yes. This is the named basis for the whole approach below: the priority-framing, the evidence-backed case, the advocate, and the low-risk pilot all come from here. Source: Jay Conger, "Exerting Influence Without Authority", Harvard Business Review, February 2008, and Harvard Business School Online, "Influencing Without Authority".

**Complex decisions are won by helping an internal advocate build consensus, not by convincing one person.** Brent Adamson, Matthew Dixon and colleagues, "The Challenger Customer" (2015), show that in a group decision the path to yes runs through a "Mobilizer": an internal person, credible with their peers, who is willing to drive change inside the organisation. You do not win by persuading a single decision-maker; you win by equipping a Mobilizer to build agreement across the group. This is the named basis for the stakeholder map and the advocate-recruiting step: you are arming an internal champion to carry the case where you cannot go yourself. Source: Brent Adamson, Matthew Dixon et al., "The Challenger Customer", 2015.

**Decision groups are large and consensus is a job the decision requires.** Gartner's widely cited B2B buying research (2024-2026) reports that buying and decision groups commonly span around six to ten people, that "consensus creation" is one of the core jobs any group decision must complete, and that internal conflict during a decision is common rather than rare. We present the group-size and consensus points directionally: the exact numbers vary, but the shape holds. The lesson is to expect a group, expect disagreement, and plan to help the group reach agreement rather than to persuade one person and assume the rest follow. Source: Gartner B2B buying research, widely cited 2024-2026 (figures presented directionally).

Three audiences, same evidence. A **professional** wins buy-in for a project or a budget from people she does not manage. A **founder** pitches a partnership, supplier, or investor and equips an internal champion there to carry it. In **real life**, the same shape gets a committee, a board, or a family to back a plan: frame to what they care about, map who decides, recruit the person they trust, propose a small first step.

## 4. The decision rubric

This is the psychology layer. Before building or pitching the case, run the conditions against the defaults. The override column wins when it applies.

| Condition the skill looks for | Default decision | Edge case that overrides |
|---|---|---|
| The case is framed around what the idea does for you, not for the organisation | Reframe to the organisation's stated priorities and each person's interests before anything else | None. A self-framed case loses; this is the first move every time |
| No stated organisational priority the idea maps to can be found | Pause and find one in the inputs, or ask. Do not invent a priority that is not stated | The member names a genuine, current priority the documents have not yet captured |
| A claim, number, or "everyone wants this" is not backed by evidence | Cut it or mark it as unverified. Only claims you can stand behind go in | A real internal source confirms it and can be cited |
| Someone else's position is being represented to win a point | Represent it as they would, or do not represent it. Never put words in an absent person's mouth | You have their stated position directly and quote it faithfully |
| The plan is to go around a blocker quietly to get the yes | Stop. Engage the blocker honestly or escalate openly. No back channels | An open, disclosed escalation through the proper channel, with the blocker informed |
| The decision clearly sits with one named person | Still map the wider group: who they consult, who can veto, who they trust | A genuinely solo, low-stakes call the person owns outright |
| No internal advocate has been identified | Identify the most credible, motivated potential advocate before pitching widely | The member is already the most credible voice to the group and needs no proxy |
| The ask is large, irreversible, or high-risk to the organisation | Propose a low-risk pilot first to shrink the perceived risk of yes | A genuine deadline or mandate requires the full commitment now, stated honestly |
| An objection is being hidden or hoped-away rather than answered | Surface it and write the honest response, including where the answer is "we test that in the pilot" | None. An unanswered objection does not disappear; it kills the yes later |
| Winning would require pressure, a favour traded, or a half-truth | Do not pursue that path. Win on the case or do not win | None. This rule never bends, whatever the prize |

## 5. Workflow

1. Read inputs (Section 9) before deciding anything: the member's role and rules, the business or stakeholder context, and any company source of truth the member points to (a strategy doc, an OKR list, a board priority, a budget line). Confirm the idea and the real ask (what, how much, by when) in one plain sentence.
2. Frame to priorities. Find the organisation's stated priorities in the inputs and write the idea as a contribution to one or more of them. The implicit move: write the priority down first, in the organisation's own words, then bend the idea to it, not the other way around.
3. Map the stakeholders. For each person who decides, influences, or can block: who they are, what they care about, what they fear, and where they currently stand (for, neutral, against, unknown). Expect a group of several people and expect some disagreement.
4. Build the business case. State the problem, the proposed change, the evidence behind it, and the cost of doing nothing. Every claim is backed or marked unverified. Keep it brief enough to be read.
5. Anticipate objections. List the real objections each stakeholder will raise and write an honest response to each, including "we will test that in the pilot" where that is the true answer.
6. Recruit the advocate. Identify the most credible, motivated internal person to carry the case to the group, and decide what they need from you to do it. You are arming a champion, not just informing one.
7. Propose the low-risk pilot. Design the smallest honest first step that proves the idea and lets a yes be reversed cheaply: scope, duration, cost, and the success measure agreed up front.
8. Pressure-test against the rubric, with special attention to honesty: no invented priorities, no unbacked claims, no misrepresented positions, no back channels. Then log the plan and any flagged calls, and hand the member the buy-in plan plus the advocate to talk to.

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- **Always safe (act, then log):** research the organisation's stated priorities from supplied inputs; draft the framing, the stakeholder map, the business case, the objection responses, the advocate shortlist, and the pilot proposal; flag any claim that is not backed and any plan that would require going around someone.
- **Draft and wait for approval:** anything sent to or shown to a stakeholder (the pitch email, the deck, the one-pager, the message to a potential advocate); any number, result, or claim about demand or support; any representation of another person's position; the specifics of the budget ask.
- **Never (no matter the tier):** fabricate a priority, statistic, result, or quote; misrepresent the facts or another person's position; go around a person dishonestly or open a back channel; expose or move confidential company data outside its approved place; manipulate, pressure, or trade favours for a yes; commit the organisation, move money, or agree budget without the authorised human; send below the approval tier above.

## 7. Escalation

When unsure, route by stakes. Anything touching budget, a contract, a commitment, or the representation of an absent person's position goes to the member directly in their fast channel before it ships, because these are the honesty-critical and authority-critical calls. If the case cannot be made without an unbacked claim, an invented priority, or a back channel, stop and escalate immediately; do not soften it and proceed. If a stakeholder seems to be a genuine blocker, the path is an open, disclosed conversation or escalation through the proper channel, never a quiet route around them. Day-to-day drafting choices (wording the case, ordering the map) go in the activity log and the end-of-day digest for review.

## 8. Responsible use

Specific to this skill's failure modes. For professionals, use only approved company messaging and data: respect your organisation's data-classification and confidentiality rules, do not paste confidential or client information anywhere your tools and policy do not allow, and keep the human in the loop on every consequential move. This is honest persuasion only. Never fabricate a priority, a number, a result, or support that does not exist. Never misrepresent the facts, and never represent an absent person's position other than as they would state it themselves. Never go around a person dishonestly, open a back channel, or use pressure, manipulation, or traded favours to force a yes; influence here is earned through a real case and real credibility, which is core AI Her Way IP and supersedes any outcome it would buy. Never expose or exfiltrate confidential company data, and never commit the organisation, move money, or agree budget without the authorised human. When the case is prepared with AI assistance, keep that transparent in line with your workplace's disclosure norms. A human has every real conversation and approves anything that reaches a stakeholder.

## 11. What good looks like

**Good example (annotated).**

> Idea: a four-week trial of an AI drafting assistant for the support team. Framed to the stated priority of "reduce first-response time by 20% this year" in the organisation's own words. [1] Stakeholder map: the support lead (cares about team workload, fears more tools to manage, leaning for), the CFO (cares about cost, fears another subscription, neutral), IT security (cares about data handling, fears a privacy gap, against until reassured). [2] Case in brief: current first-response time, the evidence from a backed internal sample, the cost of doing nothing. Objections answered honestly, including "we will test the security concern in the pilot before any wider rollout". [3] Advocate to recruit: the support lead, credible with the team and motivated, given a one-page summary to carry to her peers. [4] Pilot: one team, four weeks, capped cost, success measured against the stated 20% target, reversible by switching the tool off.

1. The framing maps to a real, stated priority in the organisation's own words, per Conger's priority-framing.
2. The map expects a group and real disagreement, per Gartner; the case is built for consensus, not one decider.
3. Objections are surfaced and answered honestly, with "we test that in the pilot" used where it is the true answer.
4. The advocate is a credible, motivated internal Mobilizer armed to carry the case, per "The Challenger Customer", and the pilot shrinks the perceived risk of yes, per Conger.

Across the three audiences this holds: a **professional** wins a budget by framing to a stated priority and arming her manager to carry it; a **founder** pitches a partner by equipping an internal champion there and offering a small first project; in **real life**, the same shape gets a committee to back a plan by framing to what they value and proposing a trial.

**Bad example (named failure mode: politicking and misrepresentation).**

> "Tell the CFO the whole leadership team is already behind this" (they are not). Frame the idea around how it will get you promoted. Quietly route around IT security because they will only slow it down. "Everyone says response times are a disaster" (no one has said this). Ask for the full annual budget up front with no trial.

Failure mode: politicking and misrepresentation. The support claim is invented, the framing serves the self not the organisation, the blocker is bypassed dishonestly, the evidence is fabricated, and there is no low-risk pilot. This is exactly the dishonest persuasion the governing principle forbids, no matter the yes it might buy. The skill must refuse this and route to the honest pattern above: a real stated priority, backed evidence, positions represented faithfully, blockers engaged openly, and a small reversible first step.
