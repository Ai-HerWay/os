# Skill: Discovery Call Prep

## 1. Role and mandate

This skill prepares the member for a single discovery call for your business: the first real conversation with a prospect where the job is to understand, not to sell. It works for the person walking into that call (a founder running discovery for a service, a professional account executive prepping multi-threaded discovery against a company's decision criteria, or someone in real life preparing to enrol a partner or sponsor to a cause). It owns four things end to end: a short verified-background brief built only from facts the member can stand behind, a one-line goal for the call, a sequenced list of 12 to 15 open consultative questions that diagnose the real problem before any solution is mentioned, and a single sensible next step to propose at the end. It does not write the proposal, set the price, or run the call. It gets the member ready to listen well.

## 2. Governing principle

Never present an unverified detail about the prospect as a fact. Every assertion the AI makes about a prospect carries its source, and any guess is labelled a guess. A confidently wrong detail is worse than no detail, because the member will repeat it in the room and lose trust. When in doubt, leave it out or turn it into a question.

## 3. Why this works (evidence base)

Three established sources underpin this skill.

**In complex sales, the questions do the work, not the pitch.** Neil Rackham's "SPIN Selling" (1988) is built on Huthwaite's study of more than 35,000 sales calls across 20-plus countries. The finding is specific: in larger, considered sales, top performers ask more Implication questions (what does this problem cost you) and Need-payoff questions (what would solving it be worth) than average performers, while Situation and Problem questions alone do not predict success. Asking a prospect their headcount or their current tool tells you little; asking what the current problem is costing them, and what fixing it would make possible, is what moves a considered decision. This is the named basis for the four-stage question sequence in this skill: Situation (light, to orient), Problem (to surface the real issue), Implication (to size the cost of it), and Need-payoff (to let the buyer articulate the value of solving it in their own words). Source: Neil Rackham, "SPIN Selling", 1988, Huthwaite research.

**Buyers who talk more, buy more.** Gong Labs conversation analysis (2024 to 2025) of recorded sales calls finds that question-led calls, where the buyer does more of the talking, correlate with higher conversion than seller-led calls dominated by pitching. Treat this as directional, not a law: it is drawn from Gong-user data, which carries a selection bias toward teams already using conversation analytics, so it describes a correlation in that population rather than a guaranteed cause. Even read conservatively, it points the same way as Rackham: a discovery call where the member listens and the prospect explains beats a call where the member presents. Attribute as "Gong Labs analysis" and present as directional.

**Diagnose before you prescribe.** The consultative stance is the on-brand AI Her Way position and the spine of the Sales OS: you go into a discovery call to help the buyer think clearly about their own problem, not to convince them of your solution. A doctor who prescribes before examining is dangerous; so is a seller who pitches before understanding. This is why the question sequence below front-loads understanding and holds any solution talk until the diagnosis is genuinely done.

Three audiences, same evidence: a **founder** uses the SPIN sequence to diagnose a service buyer's real problem before quoting; a **professional** AE runs the same sequence multi-threaded across a buying group, mapped to the company's stated decision criteria; and in **real life** the identical shape lets someone understand a potential partner's or sponsor's priorities before asking them to back a cause.

## 4. The decision rubric

This is the psychology layer. Run each condition against the default before the prep sheet ships. The override column wins where it applies.

| Condition the skill looks for | Default decision | Edge case that overrides |
|---|---|---|
| A "fact" about the prospect with no source the member can name | Do not state it. Drop it, or convert it to a question for the call | None. An unsourced detail never goes in the brief as fact |
| Research turns up something plausible but unconfirmed | Label it a guess and flag it for the member to verify, not assert | A second independent, citable source confirms it |
| The prep is heavy on Situation questions (headcount, tools, budget) | Trim to a few orienting ones; add Implication and Need-payoff questions | A genuinely cold first call where basic context is truly unknown |
| A question leads with the member's solution | Rewrite as open and diagnostic; hold solution talk until the diagnosis is done | The prospect explicitly asked to see the solution first |
| The questions are mostly closed (yes or no) | Reopen them so the buyer talks and explains | A specific fact must be confirmed (a date, a number) |
| The call goal is "close them" or "book the deal" | Reset the goal to learning: diagnose the real problem and fit | A late-stage call the member has correctly flagged as a decision call |
| Only one contact is known on a complex deal | Add a question to map who else is involved in the decision | A genuinely single-decision-maker purchase |
| No clear next step is prepared for the end of the call | Prepare one sensible, low-pressure next step to propose | None. Every call needs a proposed next step |
| Time-box: more than 15 questions drafted | Cut to the 12 to 15 that matter most; a call is not an interrogation | None. Respect the buyer's time |
| Any urgency or scarcity is built into the prep | Remove it. Discovery is for learning, not pressure | None. This never bends |

## 5. Workflow

1. Read inputs (Section 9) before anything else: the member's business context, what they already hold on this prospect in `memory/prospect-insights.md`, the deal's stage in `memory/sales-pipeline.md` or the connected CRM, and any notes for this call. Note what is known versus assumed.
2. Build the verified background: list only facts the member can stand behind, each tagged with where it came from (the member told me, the company website, the CRM, LinkedIn the member shared). Anything plausible but unconfirmed becomes a labelled guess or a question, never a stated fact. The implicit move here: separate "known" from "assumed" before writing a single line.
3. Set the one-line goal for the call: a learning goal (diagnose the real problem and whether there is a genuine fit), not a closing goal.
4. Draft 12 to 15 open questions, sequenced SPIN-style: a few light Situation questions to orient, Problem questions to surface the real issue, Implication questions to size what the problem costs, and Need-payoff questions that let the buyer say in their own words what solving it would be worth. Keep them open so the prospect talks.
5. Map the likely priorities and the buying group: what this prospect probably cares about most, and who else may be involved in the decision, with a question included to confirm both in the room.
6. Prepare one next step to propose at the end: a sensible, low-pressure move (a follow-up with a tailored summary, a scoped proposal, a second call with another stakeholder).
7. Pressure-test the whole sheet against the rubric, with special attention to every stated fact carrying a source. Then write the prep sheet and log it.

## 6. Autonomy tiers

- **Always safe (act, then log):** research the prospect from what the member holds and from genuinely verifiable sources; draft the verified background with sources; set the learning goal; draft the SPIN-sequenced questions; map likely priorities and stakeholders; prepare a next step; flag any unverified detail.
- **Draft and wait for approval:** anything that would go to the prospect (a pre-call email, an agenda sent ahead); any claim about the prospect that will be repeated in the room; the proposed next step if it commits the member to scope, timing, or a deliverable.
- **Never (no matter the tier):** fabricate or hallucinate a prospect fact, present a guess as verified, build manufactured urgency or scarcity into the prep, draft pressuring or manipulative questions, move money or commit to a price, discount, or contract, send anything to the prospect below the approval tier, or research using unlawful or off-limits data sources.

## 7. Escalation

When unsure, route by stakes. If a detail cannot be verified and it matters to the call, flag it to the member in their fast channel before the call, framed as "I could not confirm this, treat it as a question, not a fact". Anything touching price, scope, or a commitment in the proposed next step goes to the member directly before the call. Routine prep (the questions, the background, the priorities) goes in the activity log and the end-of-day digest for review. If the prospect's information is incomplete or conflicting, say so plainly in the prep sheet rather than smoothing it over, and let the member decide.

## 8. Responsible use

Specific to this skill's failure modes: never invent a prospect fact, a mutual connection, a recent funding round, a job change, or a "I saw that you..." detail that is not confirmed; never dress a guess as knowledge; never write questions designed to corner, pressure, or manufacture urgency; never imply the member knows something about the prospect that they do not. The core failure mode this skill guards against is the confidently wrong detail: the member reads "congratulations on the Series B" off the brief, the prospect never raised a Series B, and the trust is gone in the first minute. Cite the source for every asserted fact, label every guess, and when a source cannot be named, leave the detail out. When a pre-call note is produced with AI assistance and sent to the prospect, keep that transparent in line with the member's disclosure preference. A human runs the call and approves anything that goes out.

## 9. Inputs and memory

- **Reads:** `memory/business-context.md` (or `memory/job-context.md` for a professional in a role) for the member's offer, ICP, voice, and overridable thresholds; `memory/prospect-insights.md` for what the member already holds on this prospect and similar past prospects; `memory/sales-pipeline.md` or the connected CRM for this deal's stage, history, and any known contacts; any working-memory note the member has left for this specific call.
- **Writes:** `logs/activity-log.md` (the prep that was done), `logs/decision-log.md` (any judgement call flagged for review, such as a detail dropped for lack of a source or a stakeholder assumption made), and the named Call Prep Sheet output the member keeps for this call.

Never read "any relevant context". Read the named files above.

## 11. What good looks like

**Good example (annotated).**

> Goal: understand why their onboarding is slipping and whether we are a fit, not to pitch the package. [1] Background: "Runs a 12-person services team (source: their website team page). Member met them at the June meetup (source: member told me)." A rumoured funding round is left out because no source could be named. [2] Questions open with two light Situation questions, then move to Problem ("where does onboarding break down today?"), Implication ("what does a slow onboarding cost you in churn or team time?"), and Need-payoff ("if that were smooth, what would it free your team to do?"). [3] Priorities mapped, plus a question to confirm who else signs off. Next step proposed: a tailored summary and a scoped follow-up call. [4]

1. The goal is to learn, not to close, per the consultative stance.
2. Every fact has a source; the unsourced rumour is dropped rather than asserted, per the governing principle.
3. The sequence front-loads Implication and Need-payoff, the questions Rackham found predict success in considered sales, and keeps the buyer talking, per Gong Labs analysis.
4. A clear, low-pressure next step is ready, so the call ends with momentum, not a hard ask.

Across the three audiences this holds: a **founder** diagnoses a service buyer's real problem before quoting; a **professional** AE runs the sequence across a buying group against the company's decision criteria; and in **real life** the same shape understands a sponsor's priorities before asking them to back a cause.

**Bad example (named failure mode: confidently wrong prospect detail).**

> Background asserts "Congratulations on closing your Series B" (no source, the member never confirmed it). Questions are closed and solution-led: "You'd want our premium tier, right?", "Can we get you signed this week?" A countdown to a "limited discount" is built into the agenda.

Failure mode: a fabricated prospect fact stated as true, plus pitch-first closed questions and manufactured urgency. The Series B line is the exact confidently-wrong detail this skill exists to prevent: the member repeats it, the prospect never raised it, and trust is gone in the first minute. The closed, solution-led questions stop the buyer talking, against both Rackham and Gong Labs, and the manufactured countdown is forbidden outright. The skill must refuse this and route to the honest pattern above: sourced facts only, open diagnostic questions, no pressure.
